*In Time* is a 2011 film set in a world where time is literally currency. Everyone is born with a clock on their arm. When the clock runs out, you die. The poor live day to day; the rich have centuries. Justin Timberlake plays a man from the ghetto who has almost no time and must steal and hustle to keep living. Olivia Wilde plays his mother, who runs out of time on a bus at 25 and dies in his arms.

The film was panned by critics. The metaphor was considered too on-the-nose.

But I want to take it seriously as a policy document — not as a warning about a distant future, but as a description of something that already exists in the present. Because the basic structure of *In Time* — where the poor have very little time left and must spend all of it to survive, and the rich have so much time they don't know what to do with it — is not a dystopian fantasy. It is a health statistic.

Life expectancy in the United States varies by zip code in ways that would be unremarkable in a science fiction screenplay. In some counties in Appalachia, average life expectancy is below 65. In some counties in Connecticut and Colorado, it exceeds 85. This 20-year difference is not primarily genetic. It is economic. It is the biological expression of who has time and who doesn't.

---

Speculative fiction about work is not usually read this way. It is read as fantasy, as warning, as entertainment, as an exploration of possibilities that haven't happened yet. I want to suggest a different reading: that the most interesting speculative fiction about work is a reading of the present, not a prediction about the future. It takes the logic of current conditions and extends it, makes it visible, removes the normalization that makes the present hard to see.

*Elysium* (2013) depicts a world in which the wealthy live on a pristine orbital station, with access to medical technology that can cure any disease, while the poor toil in polluted factories on an exhausted Earth. This is science fiction. It is also a description of the global healthcare inequality that exists right now, where your access to medicine depends almost entirely on your national and economic location.

*Parasite* (2019) is not quite speculative — it is a realist film — but it functions as dystopian fiction because it makes visible a social structure that is usually naturalized. The Kim family and the Park family exist in the same city, in the same economy, with entirely different relationships to time, space, security, and dignity. The film's horror comes not from any supernatural element but from the violence latent in the economic structure itself — the violence that surfaces when the fiction of meritocracy breaks down.

*Nomadland* (2020) is similar: not science fiction, but a documentation of a class of Americans who have been displaced by economic conditions — plant closings, retirement insecurity, the collapse of certain regional economies — and are now living in their vehicles, traveling between seasonal work assignments. The film treats this as dignity rather than disaster, which is itself a kind of political statement: these people have found a way to live, and it has its own grace. But the conditions that produced their situation are not graceful. The closure of the US Gypsum plant in Empire, Nevada, which the film depicts, ended a community in a way that no individual adaptation could fully repair.

---

What I am suggesting is that we read speculative fiction about work the way Chekhov read theatre: if a gun appears in the first act, it will fire in the third. The social and economic structures depicted in dystopian fiction — extreme stratification, the ownership of time, the casualization of all labor, the reduction of the body to an economic input — are not arbitrary inventions. They are extrapolations from tendencies already present.

The Hunger Games depicts a society organized around the extraction of resources from peripheral regions for the benefit of a center, which is more comfortable and enjoys spectacle while the periphery labors and suffers. This is not a novel social structure. It is a description of the relationship between the global South and the global North, between the exurb and the city center, between the warehouse worker and the consumer who receives their package in two days.

*The Handmaid's Tale* depicts a society in which women's reproductive labor has been nationalized — where the state controls women's bodies in order to manage the production of the next generation. This is science fiction. It is also an extension of the existing tendency to regulate reproduction — in abortion restrictions, in welfare rules that limit family size for benefit recipients, in immigration policies that separate families — in ways that treat women's bodies as resources to be managed by institutions rather than as autonomous persons.

Reading these texts as policy documents is uncomfortable, because it requires taking seriously the claim that the structures being depicted are not fundamentally different in kind from the structures we currently inhabit — just further along a continuum, or lacking the normalizing apparatus that makes the present bearable.

---

David Graeber argued that most people perform significant amounts of work that they privately believe serves no social purpose — work they would describe, if pressed, as bullshit. His book *Bullshit Jobs* documented, through testimonies from workers across sectors, the prevalence of this experience: the feeling that the work one does is not merely unpleasant or poorly compensated, but genuinely without value.

This is itself a kind of dystopia — not the dramatic dystopia of the science fiction film, but the quotidian dystopia of the meaningless day. Graeber found it not primarily in the jobs that are obviously degraded (the precarious, the low-paid, the manual) but in sectors that are conventionally considered respectable: finance, legal services, management consulting, corporate administration. The people performing these jobs were often well-compensated. They were also, a significant fraction of them, quietly convinced that the world would be no worse off if their jobs disappeared.

If that is true — if a meaningful fraction of the work being performed in modern economies is, in the judgment of the people performing it, without social value — then the dystopia is already here. Not in the form of flying cars and orbital elites, but in the form of the alarm clock, the commute, the open-plan office, and the meeting to discuss the meeting.

---

The most honest speculative fiction about the future of work does not describe a catastrophic departure from the present. It describes the present, continuing.

What it takes for the continuation to seem dystopian rather than normal is the removal of the normalization. The lifting of the veil that makes it possible to move through a system without seeing the system. The extension of the current logic just far enough that its consequences become undeniable.

The question speculative fiction asks — and that this series has been trying to ask — is: what future is already implied by the present? If we continue on the current trajectory, without intervention, without political will to change the distribution, without a genuine reckoning with who benefits and who pays — where does this go?

The dystopia is not a warning about what might happen. It is a map of where we already are, drawn by someone who refused to stop looking.
