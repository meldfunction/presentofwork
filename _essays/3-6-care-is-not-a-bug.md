There is a number that exists in economic research and is very rarely discussed in public.

The McKinsey Global Institute estimated, in 2015, that unpaid care work — the cooking, cleaning, childcare, elder care, and household management performed primarily by women, primarily without compensation — represents between $10 trillion and $39 trillion in annual economic value, depending on how it is measured. Even at the low end, this is roughly 13% of global GDP, in work that appears in no national account.

To put that differently: the global economy, as it is measured, is built on a foundation of unmeasured work that is roughly the size of the US and Chinese economies combined. This work is not a supplement to the economy. It is a precondition of it. Without the care work — the children raised, the sick tended, the houses maintained, the meals cooked, the elderly accompanied — no other work happens.

And it is not compensated. Not primarily. Not adequately. Not in any way that reflects the $10-39 trillion figure.

This is not an oversight. It is a design.

---

The feminist economists who have made this argument — Marilyn Waring, Nancy Folbre, Silvia Federici, Arlie Hochschild — have been making it for decades, and it has not achieved significant policy traction for reasons that are not mysterious. The people who set economic policy have, historically, been people for whom care work was arranged by someone else. The people who perform most care work have not historically had significant political power.

But the argument itself is straightforward, and I want to state it as plainly as possible.

If care work were compensated at the rate that economists use to value it — the replacement cost of hiring someone to perform each of its functions at market rates — then the people who perform it (primarily women, primarily poor women in wealthy countries and in the global South) would be among the most economically significant contributors in any economy. If care work were compensated at the rate that its social importance justifies — the reproduction of the labor force, the maintenance of human beings, the transmission of culture and language and values — then the conversation about compensation would be entirely different.

Instead, it is performed primarily as a gift — as an obligation of relationship, of gender, of family membership — extracted from people who have limited power to refuse it, and then described as a private matter rather than a public economic fact.

---

The paid care economy — the portion of care work that is formally compensated, in childcare, home health care, nursing, and social work — is the low-wage sector most directly shaped by the ideology that care is naturally cheap.

Home health aides in the United States earn a median wage of approximately $14 per hour. They perform among the most physically and emotionally demanding work in the economy — managing complex medical needs, providing intimate personal care, maintaining relationships with clients who are often frightened and isolated, and doing all of this in private spaces with minimal supervision and minimal support.

The low compensation is not a market failure. Markets don't fail at setting wages for home health aides; markets succeed, and they set the wage low, because: the workers are predominantly women of color and immigrants with limited labor market power; the "employer" is often a private individual (the care recipient or their family) rather than an institution with collective bargaining exposure; the "skill" involved is feminized and therefore assumed to be natural rather than learned; and the political power of the care economy has historically been minimal.

The argument that care work is low-paid because it is low-skilled deserves a direct response: it is not low-skilled. It requires technical knowledge of medical conditions and medication management; physical skill in transfers, wound care, and personal hygiene assistance; emotional intelligence in managing the fear and grief and sometimes hostility of people who are losing function; and the organizational capacity to manage complex daily routines in environments that are not designed for professional care.

The "skill" is not recognized because the work is feminized. The feminization came first, not the skill assessment.

---

What would it cost to properly compensate care work?

In the United States, the projected shortfall in the care workforce — the gap between the care workers needed to serve the aging population and the workers available to fill those positions — is enormous. The home health and personal care aide workforce needs to grow by over 1 million workers by 2030. Recruitment into these jobs at current wages is inadequate. The obvious solution — raise wages — requires a funding mechanism, because most care services are publicly funded (Medicare, Medicaid) or paid by individuals with limited resources.

The Care Economy proposal advanced by the Biden administration — a significant investment in childcare and elder care infrastructure, combined with improved wages for care workers — estimated costs in the range of $450 billion over ten years. This sounds large, and it is. It is also substantially less than the defense budget, substantially less than the cost of the tax cuts enacted in 2017, and substantially less than the $10-39 trillion in unpaid care work that the formal economy currently relies upon to exist.

The argument that we cannot afford to properly compensate care work is an argument that we cannot afford to properly compensate the work that the economy is built on.

This is not an economic argument. It is a political one.

---

The care crisis is not only about compensation. It is about the social organization of care — about who is responsible for it, who has access to it, and how it is provided.

The United States is an outlier among wealthy nations in its reliance on market provision and family (primarily maternal) provision of care, and its minimal public infrastructure for it. The OECD average public expenditure on early childhood education and care is approximately 0.8% of GDP. The US spends approximately 0.5%. The countries with the most extensive public care infrastructure — Sweden, Denmark, France — combine high maternal labor force participation with high child outcomes, suggesting that public investment in care is not a luxury but an efficiency.

The political resistance to public care investment in the United States draws on several sources: the ideology of the private family as the appropriate site of care; the racial history of public programs that have been associated with Black families and therefore politically vulnerable; the fiscal conservatism that treats public investment as inherently suspect; and the specific political economy of the care industry, which employs a politically underrepresented workforce.

These are not immovable obstacles. They have been moved, in other countries, by sustained political movements that built the power to move them.

---

Care is not a bug in the economic system that better technology will eventually fix. It is not a transitional problem that automation will resolve. The care that a frightened child needs is not substitutable. The presence that an isolated elder needs is not substitutable. The relationship that a person with dementia needs — the patient, persistent, responsive relationship with someone who knows them and meets them where they are — is not substitutable.

This is the thing about care: it is irreducibly relational. It requires a particular human being to be present to another particular human being in a way that attends to the specific person in front of them, with their specific history and their specific needs and their specific fears.

No efficiency gain makes this cheaper. No technology replaces it. Any system that pretends otherwise is not solving the problem. It is managing its visibility.

Kahlil Gibran wrote that work is love made visible. It is a beautiful phrase, and it describes something real about the experience of care work at its best — the nurse who sits with the frightened patient, the parent who gets up at 3am, the teacher who stays after, the home health aide who learns the particular way her client takes comfort.

What is not beautiful is that we have built an economy that takes this love — visible, material, exhausting, irreplaceable — and pays it at $14 an hour.

The care crisis is not a staffing problem. It is a values problem, expressed in wages. And you can tell it's a values problem rather than a resource problem by noticing which things we consistently find the money for, and which things we consistently find we cannot afford.
