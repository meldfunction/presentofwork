In 1983, the sociologist Arlie Hochschild published *The Managed Heart: Commercialization of Human Feeling*. She had spent years studying flight attendants and bill collectors — two occupations that required their workers to manage their emotional expression as a central job function.

Flight attendants were trained to be warm, patient, and cheerful regardless of how they felt. They were instructed that their smiles were a service, their calmness a product, their emotional availability a component of what the airline was selling. They were also instructed, more subtly, to manage the emotions of passengers — to absorb anxiety, defuse aggression, contain grief — without becoming visibly depleted by the effort.

Bill collectors were trained to maintain a controlled aggression — firm, persistent, affectless — regardless of what they encountered on the other end of the line. Hostility did not deter them. Grief did not soften them. The emotional constancy was the skill, deployed in service of an institutional purpose that required consistency over authenticity.

Hochschild called this "emotional labor" — the management of one's emotional expression as a job requirement, for a wage, in service of an organizational goal. And she asked a question that her successors have been trying to answer ever since: what does this cost?

---

What Hochschild found was that sustained emotional labor produces a particular kind of estrangement — not from the job, exactly, but from the feelings themselves.

Workers who had been performing warmth for years found it harder to access warmth in their private lives. Flight attendants who had been smiling professionally for a decade sometimes described difficulty feeling — not just performing — genuine pleasure. The managed heart had been managed so thoroughly that the manager had difficulty locating the thing being managed.

Hochschild called this "emotional numbing," and she was careful to distinguish it from ordinary tiredness. It was not that the workers were exhausted and needed rest. It was that the sustained performance of an emotion, as a job, had disrupted the relationship between the performance and the underlying state — had made the emotion feel, even to its performer, like a product rather than a response.

This is the specific pathology of emotional labor as work: that it takes something genuinely human — the capacity for warmth, care, patience, connection — and converts it into a professional resource. A resource that can be deployed, managed, trained, and evaluated. And in the conversion, something changes in the relationship between the worker and their own interior life.

---

The category of emotional labor has expanded significantly since Hochschild's original study.

It is now a feature of almost every service-sector job. The barista who must be enthusiastically positive at 6am. The retail worker who must be grateful when a customer demeans them. The nurse who must be patient when a patient is frightened and hostile. The teacher who must be engaged when they are bored and exhausted. The customer service representative who must apologize for things that are not their fault, in a tone that conveys genuine regret, to people who are sometimes abusive.

The emotional labor is real. It is skilled. It requires training and practice and conscious management. And it is, in most analyses of job requirements and compensation, invisible.

When we talk about what makes a job "good" — skill required, education needed, physical demand, cognitive complexity — we typically do not include emotional complexity. A job that requires a high degree of emotional management is not automatically considered a "skilled" job by the frameworks that determine compensation. This invisibility is not accidental. Emotional labor was feminized early — associated with the caring, nurturing qualities that women were assumed to possess naturally, and therefore assumed to cost them nothing.

If emotional competence is assumed to be natural, then performing it at work is not understood as labor. It is understood as simply being a woman in a service-sector job.

This is the economic argument underneath the feminist argument about emotional labor: the systematic undervaluation of emotional work tracks, almost perfectly, the systematic undervaluation of feminized work. The jobs that require the most emotional labor are among the most poorly compensated. This is not because emotional labor is easy. It is because we have decided, culturally and economically, not to count it.

---

Emotional labor in the workplace is often discussed as a women's issue. It is also a racial issue, in a specific and underappreciated way.

Workers of color — particularly Black women — are often required to perform an additional layer of emotional management: the management of white discomfort with their presence, their competence, their assertiveness. The labor of making white colleagues comfortable, of softening directness to avoid being perceived as "aggressive," of navigating the specific double bind of being both the person who is supposed to make everyone feel at ease and the person whose confidence is perceived as threatening — this is emotional labor with no name in the standard HR taxonomy.

Scholars like Kimberlé Crenshaw and bell hooks have documented this for decades. What they are describing is not simply racism in the workplace, though it is that. It is a specific form of emotional labor imposed on workers who are already doing the baseline labor of performing professional warmth, with an additional layer of labor added by the specific dynamics of racial hierarchy.

This labor is invisible in the economy of credit, recognition, and compensation. It is very visible in the economy of exhaustion.

---

There is a concept in Buddhist thought — *mudita*, often translated as "sympathetic joy" or "appreciative joy" — that I find useful here as a contrast.

*Mudita* is the capacity to feel genuine joy at others' wellbeing — not as a performance, not as a duty, but as an authentic response that arises from a cultivated orientation toward the world. It is the opposite of envy and the complement of compassion. It is, in some traditions, considered one of the highest qualities of a developed human being.

The managed heart is, in some sense, an impoverished substitute for *mudita*. The flight attendant's professional smile is not sympathetic joy. It is a performed approximation of warmth, deployed as a service, exhausting the self that generates it rather than renewing it.

This is not the flight attendant's failure. It is the structural failure of treating a genuine human capacity as a professional resource rather than as a characteristic of a person whose wellbeing the job should support rather than deplete.

The emotional demands of caring work — nursing, teaching, social work, therapy — are real demands. They require real skill. They produce real benefit, for the people on the receiving end. The question is not whether these demands are valid. The question is what would be required to honor those who meet them in a way that sustained rather than depleted them.

---

The future-of-work discourse has a particular relationship to emotional labor that is worth noting.

When AI advocates argue that AI will take over the "routine" parts of human work and free humans for "higher-order" and "uniquely human" tasks, they typically include emotional labor in the category of "uniquely human." The nurse will not be replaced by the robot — the robot cannot provide the comfort, the presence, the genuine human contact that patients need. The therapist will not be replaced — the relationship is itself the therapy.

This is, broadly, true. And it makes emotional labor sound like a refuge.

But there is something worth questioning in this framing. The work that AI cannot do turns out to be precisely the work that has historically been most poorly compensated, most exploited, most invisible in economic accounting. The "uniquely human" work that the future-of-work discourse promises to leave to humans is, in large part, care work — and care work has consistently been treated as if it were less deserving of economic recognition than the "skilled" work that machines are taking.

The promise that the remaining human jobs will be the caring ones is not the promise of a better future for workers. It is the promise of more of what we've already had: the labor that no one wants to count, performed by the people least positioned to demand that it be counted.

Unless, this time, we count it.
