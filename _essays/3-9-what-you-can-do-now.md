I want to be honest about the limits of this piece before I write it.

"What you can do now" is a genre of writing that I have complicated feelings about. It is the genre of the productivity newsletter, the personal finance blog, the self-help bestseller — the genre that responds to structural problems with individual solutions, that addresses systemic failure by adjusting personal behavior, that lets the system off the hook by redirecting frustration toward actionable tips.

I do not want to write that piece. The problems this publication has been examining — the ideology of work, the commodification of care, the extraction of the platform economy, the structural underpayment of feminized labor — are not problems that individual choices, however conscientious, can solve. They require structural change. They require political power. They require the long, frustrating work of building movements and changing laws and redistributing the resources that are currently concentrated in the hands of very few people.

That work is the real work.

But structural change is also made of individual choices, accumulated and connected. The person who joins the union and the person who starts the cooperative and the person who votes and the person who talks to their neighbor and the person who refuses the terms they are given and asks for something different — these are not substitutes for structural change. They are its component parts.

So here is what you can do now, honestly. Not as a substitute. As a beginning.

---

If you're an employee, the most underused tool available to you is almost certainly the one you already have: the right to talk to your coworkers about money. The right to discuss your wages is federally protected under the National Labor Relations Act, and research consistently shows that workers who actually exercise it narrow the pay gaps based on race and gender that persist in nearly every workplace. The discomfort that surrounds these conversations is a social norm. It was cultivated. It serves employers, not workers.

Beyond that: find your union, or find the people in your workplace who are thinking about starting one. Union membership is at a historic low; union organizing is at a historic high. Starbucks workers, Amazon workers, graduate students, media workers — the movement is real and it is growing, and it is growing precisely because people in workplaces without unions decided the discomfort of organizing was worth it. The AFL-CIO and SEIU and most sector-specific unions have resources specifically for workers in non-union workplaces. Using those resources is not revolutionary. It is the exercise of a right that people died to establish.

If you're a freelancer or independent contractor, the most consequential thing you can do is build direct relationships — email lists, direct contracts, connections that survive platform migration — so that your livelihood doesn't depend entirely on infrastructure you don't own and could be removed from without notice. The second most consequential thing is to price your time as if it includes what you are not being paid for: health insurance, retirement, vacation, sick days, and the business development time that are built into the compensation of employees. Pricing below that rate is subsidizing the client's decision to avoid the costs of employment.

If you run a business or organization, pay fairly — which requires knowing what fair means, what a living wage is in your area, what pay equity requires — and be transparent about wages within the organization. The discomfort that transparency produces is information. It tells you where the justifications don't hold. That discomfort is a feature. And if you're a founder approaching retirement or exit, the question of converting to worker ownership is worth taking seriously. It is not exotic. The National Center for Employee Ownership and the Democracy at Work Institute support this work specifically.

As a citizen: the structural changes that would most substantially improve the conditions of work — labor law reform, care infrastructure investment, platform regulation, cooperative development support — are political questions with political answers. They require the sustained, boring, essential work of showing up. And how you spend money influences which models survive. Cooperatives, union shops, worker-owned platforms — choosing these when you have the choice is not a solution to structural problems, but it is solidarity, and it matters to the people whose livelihoods it touches.

---

I want to close with something that is not on the list, because it is not an action item but it is, in my view, the most important thing.

The work this publication has been trying to do is not primarily to provide a program. It is to change the frame — to make visible the assumptions that keep the current order in place by making it seem natural and inevitable, and to suggest that the order is neither natural nor inevitable, that it was built and can be built differently.

What you can do now, beyond any specific action, is carry that awareness into the places where you spend your time. Into the meeting where the decision is being made. Into the conversation where someone is justifying the current arrangement as the only possible one. Into the moment where you have some small degree of choice about how to act, and the choice matters to someone.

The present of work is being made, right now, by the choices that real people make in real institutions about what is acceptable and what is not. About what they will ask for and what they will accept. About who they will organize with and what they will organize for.

You are inside the present of work.

What you do in it is not nothing.
