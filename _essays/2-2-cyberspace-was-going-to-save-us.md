John Perry Barlow published "A Declaration of the Independence of Cyberspace" in February 1996, from Davos, Switzerland, of all places, on the occasion of the signing of the Telecommunications Decency Act.

"Governments of the Industrial World," it began, "you weary giants of flesh and steel, I come from Cyberspace, the new home of Mind. On behalf of the future, I ask you of the past to leave us alone."

It continues in this vein for a while — about how cyberspace has no geography, no physical coercion, no bodies to govern, how it is "a world where anyone, anywhere may express his or her beliefs, no matter how singular, without fear of being coerced into silence or conformity," how "the global conveyance of thought" would no longer need the state.

Barlow was not a fringe thinker. He was a Grateful Dead lyricist and a founding member of the Electronic Frontier Foundation. He was expressing, with unusual elegance, what was at the time genuinely close to a mainstream position in the tech world: that the internet was a fundamentally liberating force, that its very architecture resisted centralization and hierarchy, that it would flatten the information landscape and give voice to the powerless, that it was, in a word, revolutionary.

He was describing something real. He was also describing something that would be true for about ten years, and then be systematically dismantled by the same market forces that had dismantled every previous promise of technological liberation.

---

The early internet was, in important respects, what Barlow said it was.

In the 1990s and early 2000s, the internet was genuinely decentralized in a way it has since ceased to be. There was no dominant platform. There were thousands of websites, message boards, mailing lists, and IRC channels, none of which had a significant fraction of total traffic. There was no algorithm deciding what you saw. There was no advertising system harvesting your attention and selling it to the highest bidder. There was no corporate landlord owning the infrastructure of public life.

The early internet also created genuine changes in the labor market for knowledge workers. Geographic barriers to employment fell — or began to fall. A programmer in Bangalore could work for a company in San Jose. A translator in Warsaw could work for a publishing house in New York. The information that had been concentrated in expensive credentialed institutions began to diffuse — Wikipedia was founded in 2001, and whatever its limitations, the fact that a comprehensive encyclopedia was freely available to anyone with an internet connection was a genuine democratization.

For freelance knowledge workers especially, the internet created real new possibilities: access to global markets for their skills, tools for marketing and communication, platforms for distributing creative work without going through the bottleneck of publishers and record labels and studios. The promise of the "long tail" — Chris Anderson's 2004 argument that the internet would allow niche producers to find niche audiences at global scale — was not entirely false.

---

What happened next is the story of every technological promise: the market found the technology, and the market shaped it.

The advertising model arrived. It was not inevitable — Tim Berners-Lee, who invented the World Wide Web, has spent decades arguing that the web's problems trace to the decision to make advertising its economic foundation. The advertising model required scale, and scale required centralization, and centralization produced the platforms.

Google. Facebook. Amazon. Apple. The five fingers of the new digital economy were not simply businesses. They were infrastructural: the roads, post offices, town squares, and shopping malls of digital life, controlled by private corporations, optimized for engagement and revenue, governed by terms of service rather than democratic accountability.

The question that Barlow's declaration did not ask — who owns the infrastructure of cyberspace? — turned out to be the question that determined everything. The infrastructure is owned by shareholders. The value produced in cyberspace — the content, the relationships, the attention, the data — flows primarily to the infrastructure owners. The people who produce the content that makes the platforms valuable are mostly not compensated for producing it. They are the product, in the famous phrase. Their attention, their behavior, their social networks are what the platforms sell.

This is not the liberation of labor. It is a new form of its extraction.

---

The gig economy is, in significant part, a product of the internet's unfulfilled promises.

The promise: technology will allow workers to access global markets for their skills, to find clients anywhere, to work for themselves rather than for corporations, to achieve the autonomy that employees have historically lacked. The reality: platforms concentrate market power in the hands of their operators, use algorithmic management to discipline workers without the legal responsibilities of employment, and compete workers against each other globally in ways that relentlessly drive down rates.

Upwork and Fiverr have turned global knowledge worker markets into a race to the bottom in which the lowest bidder wins, and the lowest bidder is usually someone in a country where the cost of living allows them to survive on rates that no worker in a wealthy country can match. The global marketplace for freelance work has not primarily benefited workers — it has primarily benefited clients.

TaskRabbit, Uber, Airbnb, DoorDash — the sharing economy promised to unlock idle resources, to allow people to monetize underutilized assets and time, to create peer-to-peer exchanges that bypassed corporate intermediaries. It did, for a moment. Then the intermediaries arrived, scaled up, captured the market, and extracted rent from every transaction between peers.

The revolution was monetized. And monetization, at scale, looks like concentration.

---

There is something worth preserving in Barlow's vision, though, that should not be lost in the critique.

The decentralized internet is not a fantasy. It is a choice that was made and can be unmade. Federated social networks — Mastodon, BlueSky's AT Protocol, ActivityPub — are attempts to build social infrastructure that cannot be owned by a single corporate entity. Cooperative platforms like Up&Go (a cleaning worker cooperative that owns its booking platform) and Stocksy (a photographer-owned stock photo cooperative) demonstrate that the platform model does not have to be extractive. Open-source software, academic publishing in preprint form, Wikipedia — these are examples of digital commons that function, that have users who value them, that prove the possibility of the internet Barlow was describing.

The digital commons is not a historical artifact. It is an ongoing experiment in organizing collective knowledge production in a way that doesn't require a shareholder to capture the gains.

What it requires is intention. The extractive internet did not arise because extraction is the only model. It arose because extraction is the model that attracts capital, and capital is what built the infrastructure. A different internet — the one Barlow was imagining, the one Berners-Lee keeps insisting is still possible — requires a different theory of who builds and maintains the infrastructure, and for whom.

This is a political question. It is also a design question. And it is still open.

---

Barlow's declaration was not wrong in its aspirations. It was naive in its mechanism. It assumed that the decentralized architecture of the early internet would protect its decentralized spirit — that the technology itself would resist the concentration of power that has characterized every previous infrastructure.

It didn't. The technology is neutral in that specific sense: it can be used to concentrate power or to distribute it. Which of those outcomes you get depends on the political economy of the people who build and own it.

Cyberspace did not save us. Cyberspace was saved from us — or rather, from the version of us that believes that human freedom can be guaranteed by the right protocol, that liberation is an engineering problem, that you can build a revolution without attending to the question of who owns the land it stands on.

The internet gave us something genuinely valuable, and something genuinely dangerous, and we are still figuring out which is which.
