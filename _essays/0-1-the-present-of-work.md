Someone you know is good at their job and hates it.

Maybe it's you. Maybe it's the nurse who gives everything at the hospital and comes home unable to feel anything. The software engineer building something he knows is making the world marginally worse, trading his days for a number in an account he barely looks at. The teacher who became a teacher because she loves how minds open, and who now spends most of her time on compliance documentation.

Or it's the other direction: the person whose work is genuinely meaningful — the care, the tending, the sustaining of children or elders or community — and who the economy treats as if she isn't working at all. The volunteer running the thing that holds the neighborhood together. The parent who left the workforce and is now, in the official language, economically inactive.

Most people, if you ask them in the right moment, will tell you something is wrong with work. Not necessarily with their particular job — though maybe that too — but with the whole frame. With the way the question "what do you do?" functions as "who are you." With the way time feels when it belongs to someone else. With the gap between what they actually contribute to the world and what the world decides to compensate.

That gap is what this publication is about.

---

The word "work" is doing too much.

It carries labor and vocation and toil and calling and craft and drudgery and meaning and exploitation all in a single syllable, without distinguishing between them. We say "I love my work" and "I'm worked to the bone" in the same breath and mean entirely different things, and the language doesn't blink. The word holds it all: the nurse's 12-hour shift and the artist's compulsion, the Amazon picker's counted footsteps and the monk's contemplative practice, the gig driver's fifth consecutive hour and the parent's twenty-year project of raising a human being.

This is not a semantic complaint. The collapse of all these things into one word is the argument.

When we talk about "the future of work," we almost always mean: the future of paid employment. The future of the transaction in which you sell your time to an entity that uses it to produce something you often don't own. That is a specific and recent arrangement. It is not what humans have always done. It is approximately 250 years old — younger than the United States, younger than the piano, younger than the idea that a doctor should wash their hands.

Before it, people worked — of course they worked, humans have always worked — but "work" was not a discrete category separated from the rest of life. You worked in the rhythms of season and community. You worked in Hebrew, where *avodah* means simultaneously work, service, and worship, because these were not separable activities. You worked in Quechua, where *ayni* — reciprocal labor — was not a favor but a cosmological fact, a form of participation in the living world. Work and rest and ceremony and relationship were porous, bleeding into each other, governed by the texture of the day rather than the demands of the clock.

Then the commons were enclosed. The factory clock was installed. The "worker" was invented as a legal category — and as that category was built, everything it excluded became invisible. Care. Ceremony. Community maintenance. The labor of raising children and tending elders. The work of the interior life. The sacred and the communal and the contemplative — all of it outside the category. Economically inactive. Not counted.

The loss is worth grieving. Not because the past was better — it wasn't, in most of the ways we measure — but because something real was taken, and we have not stopped paying for the taking. The severing of work from relationship. The conversion of time into a commodity. The systematic devaluation of everything that couldn't be priced. These are not the inevitable costs of progress. They are choices, made at great violence to the people they were made upon, and they can — in some form, in some way — be made differently.

---

When a caterpillar dissolves inside its chrysalis, the imaginal cells activate.

They were there all along — dormant, encrypted in the caterpillar's biology — but they only become active in the conditions of dissolution. At first, the caterpillar's immune system attacks them. They are foreign bodies. Threats to the existing order. But they keep activating, and multiplying, and eventually they find each other. They form clusters. The clusters connect. And out of the dissolved caterpillar, a different kind of creature assembles itself, one that could not have been predicted from the caterpillar's form.

This is what I think is happening, right now, in the economy of work.

The worker cooperatives and the mutual aid networks and the community land trusts and the platform cooperatives — the care workers organizing in states that never thought they'd organize, the domestic workers winning bills of rights, the four-day workweek pilots and the UBI experiments and the federated social networks and the open-source ecosystems — these are imaginal cells. They look marginal. From the perspective of the dominant system, they are foreign bodies, too small to matter, attackable as naïve. But they carry a blueprint. And they are finding each other.

This publication is not neutral about that. It believes the blueprint is real.

---

Ivan Illich, writing in 1973, observed that in industrial society, human wants are no longer simply felt and acted upon — they are first converted into nouns. A need becomes a product. A desire becomes a commodity. And in that conversion, something changes in our relationship to the want itself. We stop being the subject of our own lives and become the object of a system designed to supply us.

"Not only what men do," he wrote, "but also what men want is designated by a noun."

Work has been nouned. Converted from a verb — something humans *do*, in infinite variety, in relationship, in context, for purposes that can be chosen — into a thing. A quantity. Something you have or don't have, in units that can be measured and owned.

To give work back its verb is not a romantic project. It is not a return to anything. It is an insistence on seeing the full range of what humans actually do — the ten types, the *ayni* and the *seva* and the *kammathāna* as much as the job and the career and the gig — and asking what an economy would look like if it were built around that full range rather than the narrow slice that can be priced.

This publication is an attempt to hold that question open. Not to answer it — the question is too large and too important for a single answer — but to resist the premature closure that happens when we accept, without examination, the categories we've been handed.

What are we building, when the language we use to describe human effort has no room for the effort that most makes us human?
