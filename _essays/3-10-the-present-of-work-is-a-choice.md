There is a moment in the history of every argument when the argument has to stop being an argument and become something else. A commitment. A practice. A way of living in the world that is oriented differently from the way the world is currently organized.

This publication has been making an argument. It has been arguing that work — the full range of human effort, the ten types, the *ayni* and the *seva* and the *kammathāna* as much as the job and the career and the gig — has been compressed into a single noun, priced, and distributed according to a logic that was built for specific purposes at a specific historical moment, and that those purposes and that logic are not the only ones available to us.

It has been arguing that the compression cost us something: the visibility of care, the dignity of communal labor, the recognition of interior and sacred work, the acknowledgment that most of what makes human society function is not compensated and is not counted and is not, in the dominant framework, real.

And it has been arguing that there are other models — in practice, in history, in the specific organisms of the cooperative economy and the mutual aid network and the platform cooperative and the community land trust — that demonstrate, with operational reality rather than theory, that the current arrangement is not the only possible one.

All of that is the argument.

Now I want to say something about the choice.

---

The choice is not primarily a policy choice. Policies matter — enormously — and this publication will continue to argue for labor law reform, for care infrastructure investment, for cooperative development support, for the regulation of platform extraction, for working time reduction, for universal basic income pilots. These are real and consequential policy choices with real and consequential effects on real people's lives.

But the choice I am describing is prior to policy. It is the choice to see.

To see that the domestic worker cleaning the hotel room is doing work that the economy depends on and fails to adequately compensate. To see that the mother who leaves the workforce to care for a child is not "economically inactive" but performing the most economically significant labor of all, for free, because the system requires her to. To see that the elder who can no longer work is not a burden on the productive but a person whose value was never reducible to their productivity. To see that the unpaid organizer, the mutual aid volunteer, the neighbor who checks in, the artist who makes work that will never generate income — that these are contributors to a world worth living in, and that the economy's failure to see them is the economy's failure, not theirs.

This is the choice that precedes all others: the choice to include, in your account of what matters, more than the market can measure.

---

There is a concept in systems thinking — the idea of the *paradigm*, in Donella Meadows's taxonomy of leverage points — that I have been circling around throughout this series. Meadows argues that the most powerful intervention in any system is a change in the paradigm: the shared set of assumptions, beliefs, and values from which the system arises. Not a policy change. Not a structural change. A change in what people believe is real, valuable, and possible.

The paradigm of the current work order holds several things as given:
- That economic value is the primary measure of human contribution
- That employment is the central form through which contribution occurs
- That the market is the appropriate mechanism for determining what anything is worth, including labor
- That the distribution of wages reflects the distribution of merit
- That work in the market economy is what a life is organized around, and that other forms of contribution are peripheral

None of these is a law of nature. Each is a choice, maintained by institutions, by culture, by the stories we tell about success and failure, by the policies that embody it, and by the moment-to-moment decisions of real people acting within a framework that most of them have not examined.

The present of work is not something that is happening to us. It is something we are making, every day, in the choices we make about what to value, what to reward, what to ask for, what to accept.

---

I want to say something about hope, because this series has been hard to write and I imagine it has sometimes been hard to read, and I do not want to leave it without saying what I actually believe.

I believe that humans are genuinely capable of organizing their labor differently. I believe this not as an expression of faith in human goodness — humans are complicated, and the history of labor exploitation is also the history of humans making choices — but as an observation of human variety. We have organized labor in hundreds of different ways, across thousands of cultures, over tens of thousands of years. The current arrangement is one of those ways. It is not the best one. It is not the inevitable one. It is the one we have, which is a different thing entirely.

I believe that the cooperative economy is real and growing and demonstrates something important about what is possible. I believe that mutual aid networks and community land trusts and platform cooperatives and time banks are not curiosities but experiments in governance, experiments that produce knowledge about how to build better, that accumulate the experience and the infrastructure that make the next attempt easier.

I believe that the care economy, if it were properly valued and properly compensated, would be one of the most economically significant and socially essential projects of the next several decades. I believe that the climate transition — the renegotiation of humanity's relationship to the natural world — will require forms of collective work and shared obligation that the current market framework is structurally unable to organize. These are not optional projects. They are the work of the coming generation, whether we choose to organize them well or badly.

And I believe — this is the belief that is hardest to defend and that I hold most tenaciously — that the quality of the conversation matters. That the stories we tell about work shape the choices people make about how to work. That naming things changes our relationship to them. That the noun can be given back its verb.

---

This is a publication about the present of work. The title has three meanings.

The present of work is a gift — the contribution that human effort makes to the world, in all its forms, the ten types, the unmeasured and uncounted as much as the compensated and certified.

The present of work is the current moment — the specific historical juncture at which we find ourselves, with AI and platform capitalism and a climate crisis and a care crisis and a crisis of meaning, all of them tangled together in ways that make the question of how we should organize human effort genuinely urgent.

And the present of work is a grammatical observation — that work is a verb, not a noun. That it is something people do, in relationship, in context, for purposes that can be chosen. That the nouning of it — the conversion of verb into abstraction, of doing into category — is the move that made it possible to manage and extract and price it, and that the un-nouning of it is the move that would make it possible to see it whole.

The present of work is not something that can be delivered to you in a newsletter. It is not something that can be achieved by the right policy at the right moment, though policy matters enormously. It is something that happens in the specific places where you spend your time and your effort — in the choices about what to make and how, in the choices about whose work to see and value, in the choices about what you will ask for and what you will offer freely and what you will refuse.

It is in all of this that the present of work is made.

It is still being made.

You are in the middle of making it.
