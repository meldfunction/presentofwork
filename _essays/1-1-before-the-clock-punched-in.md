Amazon's fulfillment centers track worker productivity in increments of fractions of a second. Algorithms monitor the time between scans. A worker who spends too long in the bathroom triggers an alert. The system knows how fast you move, how often you pause, whether your pace is slowing toward the end of a shift. It sets the expected rate and it measures your deviation from it, continuously, without a human supervisor needing to watch.

This is the logical endpoint of a specific way of understanding what work is: time, owned, measured to the second. It did not begin with Amazon. It began, in the form we would recognize, roughly five hundred years ago — and it began as a fight.

Here is a working day in rural England, sometime in the 1300s.

You rise roughly with the light. You work — ploughing, sowing, tending — in the fields that your village holds in common, in strips distributed by negotiation and tradition, not deed. You stop when the church bell rings for prayer. You stop when it rains hard enough to make the ground unworkable. You stop for feast days, of which there are somewhere between eighty and a hundred a year depending on the region. You stop because your neighbor needs help repairing a wall and reciprocity is not a transaction here — it is the organizing logic of a life. You stop, most days, before dark.

This is not a description of leisure. The work is brutal. The poverty is real. Childhood mortality is staggering, harvest failure is a death sentence, and the legal and social constraints that govern your life are not gentle. But there is something in this description that we have since lost, that is worth naming before we go any further into history: the rhythm of labor in this world is not set by a clock. It is set by light, weather, ceremony, relationship, and the body's own limits.

The clock had already been invented. The monastery clock, designed to regulate the hours of prayer, had been ticking in European towers since the 13th century. But it had not yet colonized the organization of labor. That colonization would come.

---

The question I want to open here is not "was the medieval peasant better off?" That is a trap. The answer is almost certainly no, in most of the ways we measure. Life expectancy, nutrition, vulnerability to catastrophe — the comparisons don't favor the pre-industrial world.

The question is different: what was the relationship between human beings and their work, before that relationship was restructured by industrial capitalism? What did it mean to work when work was not yet a category — when it was not separable from rest, from community, from ceremony, from the meaning a person made of their days?

The answer is not one thing. There is no single pre-modern relationship to labor. The Quechua peasant's *ayni* — reciprocal labor that operates without ledger or time horizon, because the relationship is itself the point — is not the same as the African communal farming practice that would later be called, dismissively, "subsistence." Neither is the same as the Japanese *shokunin*'s devotional pursuit of craft mastery, or the sacred work of the medieval stonemason who understood their labor as liturgy. These are radically different relationships to effort and time and meaning.

What they share — and what distinguishes them from the dominant modern arrangement — is that work in these contexts was not yet fully severed from the relationships in which it occurred. The work and the worker were not yet abstracted from each other.

That abstraction is the story we're tracing.

---

The historian E.P. Thompson, writing in 1967, made a distinction that is worth carrying through this entire series. He distinguished between task-oriented labor — where the shape of the work is determined by what needs to be done — and time-oriented labor, where the shape of the work is determined by the clock.

In task-oriented labor, you finish when the task is finished. There is a natural unit — the ploughing of a field, the completion of a pot, the landing of the fish. Time is a byproduct. In time-oriented labor, you finish when the clock says. The task is a byproduct. Time is the thing being sold.

This is not a small shift. It is a restructuring of the fundamental relationship between human beings and their effort. Under task orientation, the question is: what needs to be done? Under time orientation, the question is: how much of your time can I purchase?

The Industrial Revolution did not merely change how work was done. It changed the ontology of work — what work *is*, what it is made of, what unit it comes in.

The unit it now came in was the hour. And the hour could be owned.

---

Before we get to the mills, though, a pause. Because the story of pre-industrial labor is not only a story of lost wholeness. It is also a story of extraordinary violence.

The feudal system that governed most of European life was not labor-as-relationship in any romantic sense. Serfs were bound to land they did not own, serving lords they did not choose, with virtually no legal recourse when that service became exploitation — which it consistently did. The enclosure of the commons, the process by which the shared land that sustained peasant communities was converted into private property and fenced off, was not an accident or an unfortunate side effect of progress. It was a deliberate act of dispossession, conducted over several centuries, that created the conditions for industrial capitalism by creating a class of people with no land and no means of subsistence — and thus no choice but to sell their labor.

What was lost in this transition was not an idyll. It was a specific material and social arrangement that, despite its many violences, included things that industrial labor would not: a degree of control over the rhythm and shape of one's working day, a relationship to the tools and land one used, a communal structure of mutual support, and — this matters — the absence of the particular form of alienation that Karl Marx would later describe as the separation of worker from product, from process, from fellow worker, and from species-being.

Alienation is not just feeling bad at work. It is a specific structural condition in which the things you make do not belong to you, the process of making them is controlled by someone else, the people you work alongside are competitors as much as colleagues, and the relationship between your effort and your humanity is severed at the point of transaction.

That severance begins here. Not with a single event, but with the slow and violent dismantling of the conditions under which something different was possible.

---

There is a concept in Quechua thought called *Pachamama* — Mother Earth, or more precisely, the living web of time and space and relationship that constitutes the world. Labor in many Andean traditions was understood as participation in Pachamama — not extraction from it. The work of farming was not the application of human will to inert matter. It was a reciprocal relationship with a living system that had its own agency and its own claims.

This is not mysticism, exactly. It is an ontology. A way of understanding what the world is made of, and what the human relationship to it consists of. And it is one that makes the concept of "labor productivity" — output per unit of input, efficiency maximized, time optimized — literally incoherent. You cannot be "productive" in relation to Pachamama. You can only be in relationship with it, and the quality of the relationship is the point.

I am not suggesting that Andean agricultural practice is the model for a 21st-century economy. I am suggesting that the existence of this ontology — and the thousands of others like it across human history and geography — tells us something important: the relationship between human effort and the natural and social world in which it occurs is not given. It is constructed. And it has been constructed very differently, in ways that reflect very different values and very different distributions of power and benefit.

The construction we live inside now was built in a specific place, at a specific time, for specific purposes — many of them related to the extraction of surplus from people who were not in a position to refuse.

That is the history of the present. It begins here, in the fields before the clock, in the moment before the separation.

It begins with the fact that there was a before.
