Here is the argument in its strongest form.

Every previous wave of automation displaced workers from specific tasks, which sounds bad but turned out to be fine, because the economy found new things for the displaced workers to do. The handloom weavers became factory workers. The factory workers became service workers. The service workers became knowledge workers. Each transition was painful for those living through it, but in the aggregate, new jobs appeared to replace the ones destroyed. The economy absorbed the displaced and found new uses for human labor.

Artificial general intelligence — the systems that can perform any cognitive task a human can perform, given sufficient training data and compute — breaks this pattern. Previous automation was narrow: machines that were better than humans at specific, defined tasks. AGI would be a machine that is better than humans at *learning* new tasks — which means that each new application does not require a new round of narrow development. You train the system once, and it generalizes.

If this happens — and there is genuine, non-fringe expert debate about whether and when — the historical relationship between automation and job creation breaks down. Because the thing humans were always doing when they found new jobs after the old ones were automated was learning: acquiring the new skills that the new economy required, adapting to the changing demands of the market. If machines can learn faster and cheaper than humans, then the humans who learned their way out of previous automation waves have nowhere to learn to.

This is the argument that "this time is different." I don't know if it's right. I'm not sure anyone does.

---

What I want to do with this uncertainty is sit in it for a moment, rather than resolving it in either the optimistic or the pessimistic direction.

The optimistic resolution: AGI doesn't happen, or happens slowly, or the jobs it creates are more numerous than the jobs it destroys, or humans prove to have forms of value that are genuinely unreplicable. The pessimistic resolution: AGI happens, wages for most workers collapse, inequality becomes extreme, we end up with a small technical-managerial class and a large economically marginalized majority.

Both of these resolutions have the same problem: they assume that the outcome is determined by the technology, rather than by the political and economic choices made in response to it.

The technology is a fact — or will be. What we do with it is a choice.

---

The economist Anton Korinek has a framework he calls "the bottleneck argument" that is useful here.

Workers have historically had economic value because they were the bottleneck in the production of goods and services. Machines could do certain things, but they could not do the things that required human judgment, creativity, and adaptability. That bottleneck — the indispensable human — is what gave workers bargaining power. It is why employers had to negotiate rather than simply replace.

If AI removes the bottleneck, the bargaining power collapses. Not immediately, not everywhere, not for all workers equally. But structurally. The leverage that came from being irreplaceable evaporates when you are replaceable.

Korinek's point is not that this is inevitable or good. It is that this structural shift, if it occurs, requires structural responses — not individual ones. The worker who "upskills" to stay ahead of the automation is performing a desperate adaptation within a structure that will continue to shift. The worker who organizes, who advocates for labor law reform, who supports political movements that would change the distribution of AI's gains — that worker is engaging with the actual problem.

---

There are several policy responses to the possibility of severe AI displacement that are actually being discussed by people who study this.

**Universal Basic Capital (UBC)**, distinct from Universal Basic Income (UBI): rather than redistributing income from those who earn a lot to those who earn little, UBC would ensure that every person at birth received ownership in a basket of productive assets. If the machines are generating the value, make sure everyone owns some of the machines. This is not a radical idea — it has been advocated by economists across the political spectrum. It is radical only in that it would require breaking the current concentration of capital ownership.

**Robot taxes**: if automation allows employers to replace human workers without any obligation to the workers being replaced, they externalize the cost of displacement onto the workers and onto public social insurance systems. A tax on automation — proportional to the labor cost avoided — would internalize some of that cost and fund the social supports that displaced workers need.

**Working-time reduction**: if the total amount of human work that needs to be done decreases, the humane response is to distribute the reduction across the workforce — shorter hours, longer vacations, more time for the things that AI cannot do — rather than concentrating the reduction among those who are fully unemployed. The four-day workweek experiments in the UK and Iceland found that productivity was maintained or improved when hours were reduced. Reducing hours is not productivity theater. It is a serious policy option.

**Platform cooperativism**: if the AI systems that replace human workers are owned by a small number of corporations, the gains flow to shareholders. If those systems were owned more broadly — by workers, by the public, by cooperative structures — the gains would be distributed differently. This requires governance of AI development that we don't currently have.

---

But I want to stay a little longer with the deeper question, which the policy responses don't fully address.

If work is where most people find identity, meaning, social connection, and a sense of contribution to the world — and for many people it is, even if it is also a site of exploitation and exhaustion — then what happens when work becomes substantially less available?

This is a question that our culture is not equipped to answer, because our culture has made the answer to the question "who are you?" almost synonymous with the answer to the question "what do you do?"

If AGI displaces enough work that full employment is no longer achievable through conventional economic policy — and this is a contested empirical claim, but one worth taking seriously — then we need not just new economic policies. We need a new cultural relationship to work. We need to find ways to affirm the value of human lives that do not depend on those lives being productive in the economic sense.

This is not easy. The Protestant work ethic is not easily reformatted. The psychological and social functions that work serves — structure, purpose, identity, belonging — don't automatically transfer to leisure. The communities organized around work — the union hall, the workplace, the professional network — don't automatically reconstitute around alternative institutions.

But the question has been asked before, in contexts where the economic necessity of most people's labor was genuinely in question. The medieval aristocrat who never did paid work had to find meaning elsewhere — in patronage, in war, in religion, in the management of estates. The monk who withdrew from the economy entirely had to find structure and purpose in the hours of prayer and the practice of the Rule. These are not models I'm proposing to replicate. They are evidence that humans can organize meaningful lives around things other than waged labor.

What those lives look like in a world of abundant AI is genuinely unknown.

That unknowing is the beginning of a real conversation, rather than the end of it.
