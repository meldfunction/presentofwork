Amazon warehouse workers are injured at roughly twice the rate of other warehouse workers in the United States. In 2022, the company reported over 38,000 serious injuries — a number that campaigners argue understates reality because of the company's incentives to report light. The injuries are predictable: musculoskeletal damage from the speed and repetition of the work, exacerbated by the algorithmic productivity monitoring that penalizes slowing down even when your body is signaling that slowing down is necessary.

The company notes that safety is a priority. This is probably true. It also employs a team of engineers whose job is to find ways to make the work faster.

This is an old tension. William Blake wrote the phrase in 1804, in the preface to a long prophetic poem called *Milton*. "Dark satanic mills" did not, in Blake's original intention, refer to the factories already spreading across the English Midlands. It referred, most likely, to the mechanistic rationalism of Newton and Locke — the mills of the mind, grinding the living mystery of the world into quantities and causes.

But the factories took the phrase, because the factories earned it.

What happened in the English textile mills of the early 19th century was not merely a change in how cloth was made. It was the forcible restructuring of the human relationship to time, to the body, to craft, to other people, and to the products of one's own effort. It was, in the compressed violence of a few decades, the conversion of a population that had organized its labor by season and task and relationship into a population that organized its labor by the clock, the whistle, and the threat of destitution.

The machinery was not the main event. The discipline was.

---

The factory clock did not just tell time. It enforced it.

Before the factory, "a day's work" was a variable quantity. It expanded and contracted with the season, the weather, the task, the social calendar. Weavers working on hand looms at home worked when they chose to work, producing as much or as little as their circumstances required, taking Monday off if they felt like it — a practice so common among English weavers that "Saint Monday" was the colloquial name for it. This drove employers and moralists to equal fury.

The factory ended Saint Monday. It ended variable hours. It ended the weaver's relationship to the full arc of the textile production process — the selection of fiber, the preparation, the spinning, the weaving, the finishing. These were now divided among dozens of workers, each performing a single repetitive motion for twelve or fourteen hours a day, in a room full of noise and dust and moving parts that could and regularly did kill them.

E.P. Thompson's great essay "Time, Work-Discipline, and Industrial Capitalism" documents what happened to the human sense of time under this pressure. Workers who had grown up with a fluid, task-oriented relationship to their hours were suddenly required to perform time as a commodity — to sell it in equal, measurable units, to appear on schedule regardless of season or mood or body, to have their time owned.

The clocks were an instrument of this change as much as they were a record of it. Factory owners installed clock towers visible from the workers' homes. Some employers were documented as having advanced their clocks at the end of the shift and set them back at the start, effectively stealing minutes each day in a way that was nearly impossible to prove.

Time had become property. And property could be stolen.

---

The body bore the cost of this conversion in ways that are almost impossible to overstate.

Child labor in the early factories was not incidental. Children were preferred for certain tasks — crawling under moving machinery to clear jammed fibers, climbing inside chimneys — precisely because their bodies were small enough to fit where adult bodies could not. The Factory Acts of the 1830s and 1840s, passed over fierce employer resistance, documented in testimony before Parliament what this meant in practice: children of five and six years old working shifts of twelve to sixteen hours, suffering injuries from machinery at rates that would now be classified as industrial homicide, growing up with spinal deformities from the positions their work required.

The adult workers fared somewhat better, in the sense that they survived longer. But the diseases of the early factories — cotton dust destroying lungs in a condition now called byssinosis, the lead and mercury poisoning in the hat-making and battery industries, the silicosis of the mines — were occupational diseases in the precise sense: diseases that the occupation produced, that the body paid for with years of life and quality of life, in exchange for wages that were set by an employer who held essentially all the structural power.

What there was no accounting for, in any wages, was the alienation.

---

Marx's concept of alienated labor is often misread as a purely economic complaint — workers don't get paid enough, the surplus goes to the owner, the laborer is exploited. This is true, but it is not the whole of what Marx was describing. He was describing a structural condition, not a grievance.

Alienated labor, in Marx's analysis, has four dimensions. The worker is alienated from the product of their labor — it does not belong to them, they have no ongoing relationship to it, it leaves their hands and enters a circuit of exchange in which they play no further part. The worker is alienated from the process — they do not control how or when or at what pace they work; these are determined by the machine and the manager. The worker is alienated from other workers — instead of a community of mutual support and shared identity, they are competitors for the same jobs, whose collective interests are structurally opposed to their individual survival in a race to accept lower wages. And the worker is alienated from their own species-being — from what it means to be a human animal whose conscious, purposeful making of the world is precisely what distinguishes them from every other form of life.

In other words: the factory did not just change where and how people worked. It changed the relationship between people and the meaning of their effort.

This is the thing that Blake had right, even if his target was the mechanistic philosophy rather than the factories themselves. The dark satanic mills were mills of the mind before they were mills of the body. They required people to experience themselves as inputs — as factors of production, as units of labor, as interchangeable components in a system whose purpose was not their flourishing but the extraction of surplus.

---

The resistance to this was immediate, sustained, and often violent.

The Luddites — whose name has been so thoroughly misappropriated by contemporary usage that it now means "someone who doesn't like new technology" — were skilled textile workers who destroyed machinery not because they feared technology per se, but because the machinery was being used to replace their skilled labor with cheaper, less-skilled labor, driving down wages and destroying their communities. They were not anti-technology. They were pro-craft. They were defending a specific relationship to work against a specific economic attack.

The machine-breaking of the 1810s was put down by military force. The movement's leaders were hanged or transported. The history is a reminder that the "natural" transition to industrial capitalism was not natural at all — it was enforced, at every stage, by legal violence and military power against people who understood very clearly what was being taken from them.

What was being taken was not just a wage. It was a relationship: to skill, to community, to the material world, to time.

---

I want to end with something that is not often included in histories of industrialization, because it does not fit neatly into narratives of progress or exploitation.

Some workers loved the factories.

Not most. Not the children. Not the ones destroying their lungs in the cotton dust. But some workers — especially those who had grown up in rural poverty so grinding that even a factory wage felt like a step upward, even a regimented schedule felt like a form of belonging — experienced the early industrial town as a kind of freedom. Freedom from the authority of the lord. Freedom from the isolation of the countryside. Freedom to exist in a city, with its markets and music halls and political meetings and sheer density of human contact.

This is worth holding alongside everything else.

The story of the dark satanic mills is not simple. It is a story in which genuine goods — economic mobility, urban life, access to the market, a wage that belonged to the earner rather than the lord — were delivered at costs that were distributed wildly unevenly: paid by the body of the mill worker, by the childhood of the chimney sweep, by the community of the village that was enclosed, by the continents of people whose labor and resources fed the whole machine without being part of the bargain at all.

Progress is real. Its distribution is not natural.

That gap — between what industrialization produced and who paid for it — is where the present of work begins.
