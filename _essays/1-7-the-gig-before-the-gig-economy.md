The word "flexibility," in labor economics, has two meanings that are rarely distinguished. There is the flexibility of the worker — the ability to set your own hours, to decline work you don't want, to organize your time around your life rather than an employer's schedule. And there is the flexibility of the employer — the ability to hire and fire on demand, to scale labor up and down with business needs, to avoid the costs associated with permanent employment: benefits, training, loyalty, the inconvenient development of institutional knowledge in people you can't easily replace.

When a platform economy company tells you that their workers "value the flexibility," they are describing, primarily, the second kind. The worker's flexibility is real but constrained: you can work at 2am if you choose, but you cannot negotiate the rate, you cannot refuse the algorithm's decisions about which jobs you're offered, and you cannot organize with other workers to improve your conditions without risking being deactivated.

This arrangement is new in its technological implementation. It is not new in its structure.

---

Contingent labor — work that is intermittent, piecework, casual, or contracted rather than employed — has existed for as long as there has been a labor market. What is presented as the "gig economy" is, in most of its essentials, a very old economic arrangement with a new interface.

The sweated trades of Victorian England employed women and children on piecework in their homes — paid per item sewn, per box filled, per flower made from wire and silk — with no guaranteed income, no protection from rate cuts, no recourse when the merchant decided to pay less. The "sweater" was the middleman who contracted with the merchant and subcontracted to the home workers, taking a margin at each step and passing the insecurity downward. This system was exposed in the late 19th century by reformers who were shocked — or claimed to be shocked — by the conditions they found. Women working 18-hour days in their own kitchens, producing luxury goods they could never afford, for rates so low that even those hours barely sustained them.

The language used to describe this at the time is instructive. The sweated worker was described as having chosen this arrangement, as being an independent producer rather than an employee, as being her own master. This independence was the reason she had no labor protections. This independence was the reason the Factories Acts, which set minimum conditions for factory employment, did not apply to her. The "freedom" to work in her own home, at her own pace, was simultaneously the legal basis for her exclusion from the rights the factory worker had fought for.

---

The dockers of the London docks in the late 19th century were "casuals" — hired at the dock gate each morning, if and when they were needed, competing with hundreds of other men for the day's work. The practice was called "the call," and it was a daily humiliation: men gathered before dawn, employers or their representatives moved through the crowd selecting those they wanted, often on arbitrary or corrupt criteria, and those not selected went home without pay, without explanation, and with the understanding that they would be back the next morning to try again.

This system produced, among other things, the great London dock strike of 1889, which was one of the founding events of modern British trade unionism. The dockers, having nothing — no contracts, no guaranteed hours, no legal status as employees — organized around the demand for a guaranteed minimum wage, a minimum number of hours per engagement, and a standardized process for the daily call. They won, for a time. The casual system was moderated, not abolished.

It was not abolished because the casual system served the employers' needs with a precision that permanence could not. The docks were not a stable enterprise. Ship arrivals were irregular. Cargo varied in volume and type. An employer who employed a fixed workforce would pay for labor when it was not needed, and could not easily scale up when demand spiked. The casual system transferred the risk of the irregular economy from the employer to the worker. The worker bore the cost of fluctuating demand in the form of fluctuating income, while the employer paid only for labor actually consumed.

This is still, structurally, how the gig economy works. The algorithm replaces the dock foreman. The app replaces the gate. The "independent contractor" classification replaces the daily hire. But the fundamental arrangement — employer bears the demand risk, worker bears the income risk — is unchanged.

---

What has changed is the scale, the speed, and the ideology.

At scale: the gig economy encompasses not just the relatively small populations of taxi drivers and food delivery workers who are its most visible face, but the entire apparatus of freelance knowledge work — the graphic designers, the copywriters, the data analysts, the consultants, the therapists on BetterHelp, the teachers on Outschool — whose labor is organized on contractual rather than employment terms. This is a large and growing fraction of the workforce, and its growth is not incidental: it represents a deliberate choice by employers to shift labor costs and risks off their balance sheets and onto the individuals performing the work.

At speed: the app and the algorithm make contingent labor arrangements possible at a granularity that the dock gate could not achieve. The Uber driver can be working and not-working and working again within the hour. The TaskRabbit contractor can move between three different clients' homes in a day, each engagement booked and paid through the platform, none of them constituting employment. The technology makes the casualization of labor almost seamlessly frictionless — and friction, in labor relations, has historically been what created the space for worker power.

At the level of ideology: the gig economy arrives wrapped in the language of entrepreneurship, autonomy, and the rejection of the traditional employment relationship. You are not a worker. You are a small business owner. You are the CEO of your own life. This framing is not entirely false — some people genuinely prefer the freedom of contingent work — but it performs a specific function in the labor relation: it makes collective action psychologically harder, because entrepreneurs don't organize; they compete. It makes legal protection inaccessible, because the law's protections attach to employees, not to independent contractors. And it makes the internalization of risk feel like a personal choice rather than a structural imposition.

---

The history of precarious labor has another dimension that the "gig economy" framing consistently obscures: it has always been racialized.

In the United States, the division between stable, protected employment and contingent, precarious labor has tracked racial lines with remarkable consistency. The New Deal labor protections of the 1930s — the National Labor Relations Act, the Fair Labor Standards Act — excluded the occupations in which Black workers were overwhelmingly concentrated: agricultural labor, domestic work, and certain categories of service work. This was not an oversight. It was a compromise with Southern Democrats who understood that racial equality in labor rights would undermine the racial hierarchy that organized the Southern economy.

The result was a two-tier labor market: one tier with legal protections, union representation, and a path to stability; another tier, largely Black, without. That structure persists. Gig work, domestic work, agricultural work, care work — the sectors most likely to be excluded from labor protections — are also the sectors most likely to be performed by workers of color, by immigrants, by women. The "flexibility" that the gig economy celebrates has always, historically, been a flexibility for some built on the precarity of others.

---

There is something we need to say plainly, that the cheerful language of the platform economy works hard to avoid.

Precarity is not a choice. It is a condition imposed by those who can avoid it on those who cannot.

For some workers, in some moments, contingent work is genuinely preferable to a conventional job: more autonomy, more variety, a better fit with caretaking responsibilities or creative commitments or the particular texture of a particular life. These workers are real, and their preferences are legitimate.

For many more, contingent work is what you do when permanent employment isn't available, or when the only available permanent employment pays less than the freelance market, or when a disability or caregiving obligation makes the conventional schedule impossible, or when immigration status forecloses the formal employment market entirely. For these workers, flexibility is not a gift. It is a condition imposed by a labor market that has decided to offload its risks onto the most vulnerable members of its workforce.

The Uber driver who tells a journalist they love the freedom is not lying. They may also be, simultaneously, anxious about their health insurance, unable to predict their income month to month, working longer hours than they would if they had another option, and performing a contentment that the platform's rating system requires them to perform. These things can all be true at once.

The gig is not new. The framing is.
