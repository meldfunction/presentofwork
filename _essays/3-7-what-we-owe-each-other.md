The Southern African philosophical tradition offers a phrase that the economic analysis of mutual obligation consistently fails to reach: *ubuntu* — often translated as "I am because we are." Not "I am in relationship with others," which preserves the individual as the primary unit. But "I am *because* we are" — the self as constitutively relational, the individual as something that only exists in the context of the community that makes them possible.

This is not a warm sentiment about human connection. It is an ontological claim: that the category of "the individual" is downstream of, and dependent on, the category of "the community." You do not exist, and then enter into relationships. You are called into existence by the relationships.

The implications for economic obligation are significant. If this is true — if the individual's capacity to work, to earn, to create, to contribute is built on a foundation of others' labor, care, teaching, and sacrifice — then the question "what do we owe each other?" already has a structural answer, prior to any political philosophy. We owe each other the acknowledgment of the debt. We owe each other the conditions that made us possible.

The philosopher T.M. Scanlon published a book in 1998 called *What We Owe to Each Other*. Its title has since become shorthand, in philosophy, for a whole approach to moral theory — the idea that morality is fundamentally about what principles of conduct could be justified to others, what any person could reasonably reject, what we owe each other as people navigating a shared world together.

Scanlon's book is technical and careful, the work of a professional philosopher. I am going to use his title in a less technical sense: as a genuine question that the current organization of work consistently fails to answer, or answers only in the most minimal terms.

What do we owe each other in an economy?

---

The minimal liberal answer is: we owe each other not to use force or fraud, to honor our contracts, and to accept the outcomes of market exchange, however unequal, as just insofar as the process was fair. Beyond this, we owe each other nothing that isn't specifically contracted. Whatever one earns in a fair market is what one deserves. Whatever one does not earn, one does not deserve.

This is a coherent position. It is also, I think, radically insufficient as an account of what we actually owe each other as members of a society whose prosperity is built on genuinely collective production.

No one has ever built a successful business without roads, courts, an educated workforce, the rule of law, currency, public health infrastructure, a communications system, and the accumulated technological and cultural inheritance of generations who worked and discovered and built before them. The economy is not the aggregate of individual transactions. It is a commons — a shared system maintained by collective effort and collective investment, producing collective surplus that is then distributed, unequally, by the rules that governance sets.

The question "what do we owe each other in an economy?" is inseparable from the question of what we owe to the commons that makes economic life possible.

---

There are several specific things we seem, on reflection, to owe each other, that the current organization of work systematically fails to provide.

**A floor.** The idea that in a wealthy society, no one should fall below a level of material security that makes a decent human life possible — not because they have earned it through labor, but because they are human beings in a community that has resources that exceed subsistence. This is not a radical idea. It is the premise of every social democratic welfare state, and of much of Catholic social teaching, and of the Rawlsian liberal theory that any rational person behind the veil of ignorance — not knowing what position they would occupy in society — would choose to ensure a decent minimum.

What is radical, and contested, is how high the floor should be, who pays for it, and whether it should be conditional (contingent on work or job-seeking) or unconditional (a right of membership in the community). The UBI debate is, at its core, a debate about whether the floor should be conditional. The evidence from pilots is that the floor being unconditional does not produce the work disincentives that conditionality proponents predict. It does produce security, health, and — often — increased economic participation, because the person who is not desperate can make better long-term decisions.

**Recognition.** The acknowledgment that forms of contribution that do not produce a market wage are nonetheless contributions — to children, to families, to communities, to the cultural commons, to the political life of the society. The unpaid caregiver is contributing. The volunteer is contributing. The parent is contributing. The neighbor is contributing. An economy that only recognizes wage labor as contribution is not only measuring wrong. It is treating wrong — it is treating people whose contribution is real and significant as though they are economically inactive.

**A voice.** The conditions under which most people work are determined unilaterally by those with property and capital. The worker who disagrees with a policy, a direction, a way of doing things, has the freedom of exit — they can leave, if they can find something better. They have no voice — no legitimate mechanism for participating in the governance of the institution they spend most of their waking hours inside. The labor movement, at its best, was an attempt to create voice where the market provides only exit. The decline of union membership in most wealthy countries has not been replaced by any other mechanism for worker voice.

**Time.** The human claim on time — on days and weeks that belong to oneself rather than to an employer — is a precondition of everything else. You cannot be a citizen if you have no time to participate in civic life. You cannot be a parent if you have no time to be present. You cannot pursue health or learning or friendship or beauty if the hours are consumed by labor before any of these become possible. The hours of the week are finite. How they are distributed — between the requirements of making a living and the requirements of living — is a political question, and it currently has a political answer that favors employers.

---

The philosopher Miranda Fricker has a concept she calls "epistemic injustice" — injustice that occurs not in the material domain but in the domain of knowledge: the refusal to acknowledge that someone's experience is real, that their testimony is credible, that they know what they are talking about.

There is a form of epistemic injustice that is endemic in the economics of work. The worker who says their conditions are bad is told they are misremembering, or exaggerating, or that the market has determined their conditions are correct. The care worker who says the emotional labor is exhausting is told they chose this work, or that care is its own reward. The woman who says her unpaid domestic work is work is told she is confused about what work is.

These are epistemic denials — refusals to take seriously what the person knows from their own experience. And they function as a form of power: the power to determine what counts as knowledge, what counts as evidence, what counts as a valid claim.

What we owe each other includes epistemic respect: the willingness to take seriously what others know from their own lives, even when — especially when — that knowledge is inconvenient to those with power over them.

---

I want to close with something that is not an argument but an observation.

The experience of genuine mutual obligation — of being part of a community in which people reliably look out for each other, where your wellbeing is genuinely someone else's concern and theirs is yours — is one of the deepest satisfactions of human social life. It appears in descriptions of the most functional communities, the most meaningful friendships, the most sustaining family relationships, the organizations people are most devoted to.

It is not an illusion or a sentiment. It is a real social good with real effects on health, longevity, psychological resilience, and civic participation. The research on social connection and human flourishing is consistent and striking: people who are embedded in genuine mutual obligation fare better, across almost every measure that matters, than people who are isolated in the market individualism that the dominant ideology of work produces.

The question "what do we owe each other?" is not, finally, a philosophical abstraction. It is a practical question about the organization of a life — about what kind of community one is willing to build, to sustain, to belong to, and to sacrifice for.

The Southern African philosophical tradition offers a phrase that the economic analysis of mutual obligation consistently fails to reach: *ubuntu* — often translated as "I am because we are." Not "I am in relationship with others," which preserves the individual as the primary unit. But "I am *because* we are" — the self as constitutively relational, the individual as something that only exists in the context of the community that makes them possible.

What we owe each other, in an economy organized around this understanding, is not charity. It is not redistribution from the fortunate to the unfortunate. It is the acknowledgment of what is already true: that every person's capacity to work, to earn, to create, to contribute — every thing they have managed to do or become — is built on a foundation of other people's labor, care, teaching, and sacrifice that was never fully repaid.

The floor is not a gift from the successful to the struggling. It is the minimum acknowledgment of a debt that runs in every direction at once.

Whether you find this compelling depends, I think, on whether you believe you made yourself. Most people, in honest moments, know they didn't.
