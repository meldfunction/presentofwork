In 2011, *Time* magazine named "the sharing economy" one of the ten ideas that would change the world.

The vision was genuinely appealing. We live in a world of underutilized resources: cars that sit parked for twenty-three hours a day, spare bedrooms that go empty, tools used once a year gathering dust in garages. The sharing economy would unlock these resources, allow them to circulate between people who needed them, create peer-to-peer exchanges that bypassed the extractive middlemen of conventional commerce, and generate income for ordinary people from the things they already owned.

It would also, the story went, be more sustainable — fewer cars needed, fewer hotels, lower environmental footprint per unit of service delivered. It would build community — people connecting with each other, reviews creating trust, the stranger in your spare room becoming, perhaps, a friend.

By 2023, Airbnb had been identified as a primary driver of housing unaffordability in cities around the world, had removed tens of thousands of units from the long-term rental market, and had produced a class of professional short-term rental operators whose businesses bore no resemblance to the original vision of renting out a spare room. Uber had lost billions of dollars while successfully suppressing the taxi industry's wages and working conditions, and was now attempting to replace its remaining driver workforce with autonomous vehicles. The sharing economy had been superseded by a platform economy that shared very little.

---

The bait-and-switch of the sharing economy is not simply a story of corporate greed overriding good intentions. It is a story about what happens when a genuinely good idea meets capital.

The good idea: peer-to-peer exchange, unlocking idle resources, building trust networks. This idea has a long history in cooperative economics, in gift economies, in mutual aid networks. Couchsurfing — the nonprofit that predated Airbnb and offered home-sharing without financial transaction — was closer to the original vision. Freecycle, tool libraries, community gardens, car-sharing cooperatives: these are the sharing economy as it was imagined.

What happened: investors saw the market for peer-to-peer exchange and funded platforms to capture it. The platforms needed to grow fast and grow big to justify valuations that would allow investors to exit at profit. Growth required removing friction — making it easier for people to participate, which meant making it more like a conventional market, which meant introducing price signals and ratings and payment systems and professional participants who could provide reliable supply. The professionalization of supply was the death of the peer-to-peer vision.

Airbnb began as people renting air mattresses in their living rooms (the name is short for "Air Bed and Breakfast"). It became a platform for professional short-term rental operators, many of them managing dozens of properties, competing with hotels on price and hotels' standards on service, and collectively converting residential neighborhoods into de facto hotel districts.

Uber began as a car service that used smartphone technology to connect riders with professional livery drivers. The surge pricing, the rating systems, the algorithmic dispatch — these were innovations on a known model. The "sharing" was added later: UberX, allowing ordinary drivers to give rides in their personal cars, was the move that created the labor model everyone knows. And that model — no guaranteed hours, no minimum wage, no benefits, no recourse to labor law because you're a "partner" not an employee — was not the sharing economy. It was the gig economy wearing the sharing economy's clothes.

---

The platform captured the surplus.

This is the structural story. A peer-to-peer exchange between two people generates value that both parties share. When a platform mediates that exchange, the platform takes a commission — typically 15-30% of the transaction value. The platform contributes the technology, the trust system, the payment infrastructure, and the brand. These are real contributions. They are worth something. But as the platform scales, its marginal cost of handling an additional transaction approaches zero, while its commission remains constant. The value extracted by the platform grows without bound.

This is what Yanis Varoufakis calls "technofeudalism" — not monopoly capitalism, which at least involves competition between capitalists, but a neo-feudal arrangement in which the platform owner extracts rent from every transaction that passes through their infrastructure, whether they are involved in producing value or not. The Airbnb host generates the value — they own the property, they clean it, they manage the guest experience. Airbnb provides the platform. The platform takes the rent.

The feudal analogy is not merely rhetorical. In the original feudal arrangement, the lord provided infrastructure — security, justice, the use of the mill — and extracted rent from the serfs who actually worked the land. The platform provides the digital infrastructure and extracts rent from the workers who actually provide the service. The proportion of value that flows to the infrastructure owner versus the value producer has historically been a subject of intense political contestation. In the platform economy, that contestation has barely begun.

---

The people who were supposed to benefit from the sharing economy — the ordinary people with spare rooms and spare time and underutilized cars — have had a complicated relationship to the outcome.

Some have genuinely benefited. The Airbnb host in a high-demand market who supplements their income by renting their spare room when they travel is real. The Uber driver who prefers flexible hours and genuinely couldn't get a stable job otherwise is real. These are not characters invented by platform PR to justify extractive business models. They are real people whose real situations are more complicated than either the platform's promotional copy or the platform's harshest critics acknowledge.

But the structure is what it is. The person who rents their spare room is competing with professional operators who have economies of scale. The Uber driver who values flexibility is competing with other drivers who have no other options, driving down the implicit wage for everyone. The platform's interest is in supply abundance — more hosts, more drivers, lower prices for users, higher transaction volume, more commission. The individual participant's interest is in scarcity — fewer competitors, higher prices, more income. These interests are structurally opposed, and the platform holds all the power.

---

There is a version of the sharing economy that has not failed, that continues to function, that actually shares.

Cooperative platforms — where the workers or producers who use the platform also own it — exist in multiple sectors. Up&Go is a platform-cooperative for home cleaning workers: the workers own the platform, set the terms, and share the revenue. Stocksy United is a photographer-owned stock photo cooperative: photographers own shares, vote on policy, and receive dividends. The Drivers Cooperative in New York is a taxi cooperative that owns its own app.

These are not dominant market players. They are demonstrations that the technology of the platform does not require the business model of extraction. The app works the same way whether it's owned by shareholders or by workers. The difference is in the distribution: in a worker-owned platform, the surplus that the platform generates flows back to the people who create the value, rather than to those who own the infrastructure through which the value passes.

This is not a fantasy. It is a choice. It is a harder choice, because it doesn't attract the same kind of capital, because it grows more slowly, because the investors who fund most platform development don't fund models where the workers are the owners.

But it is a choice that is being made, in real organizations, with real workers, right now.

The sharing economy that was promised in 2011 still exists. You just have to look for it in places where shareholders are not the primary beneficiaries.
