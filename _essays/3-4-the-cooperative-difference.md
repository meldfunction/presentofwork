In Quechua, *ayni* is reciprocal labor. I help you; you help me. Not as transaction — there's no ledger, no timeline, no debt that accumulates and must be cleared. The relationship is the accounting. The work is an expression of the relationship, not a service rendered to a stranger in exchange for equivalents.

*Minka* is communal labor: the whole community comes together to build the road, repair the irrigation channel, raise the house for the family that lost theirs. Also without payment. Also without ledger. The contribution is to the commons, and the commons is what everyone lives inside.

These are not primitive precursors to modern economic organization. They are sophisticated answers to the question that worker cooperativism is also trying to answer: who captures the gains from collective effort? *Ayni* and *minka* say: the gains stay in the community, because the community is what made the work possible. The cooperative model, at its best, says the same thing in the language of democratic ownership and distributed governance.

I want to be honest about something at the start of this piece: worker cooperatives are not a perfect solution.

They face governance challenges that conventional companies don't. The democratic decision-making that is their defining characteristic is slower than executive decision-making, and slower is sometimes a competitive disadvantage in fast-moving markets. Member-owners can be risk-averse in ways that limit investment. Scale is difficult — the cooperative model that works beautifully at 50 people becomes complicated at 500 and very complicated at 5,000. And cooperatives operating in a capitalist market are not insulated from that market's pressures; they may internalize competition, austerity, and growth imperatives in ways that undermine their values over time.

All of this is true, and worth knowing before you decide cooperativism is the answer to everything.

What is also true is that the cooperative model answers several specific questions that the dominant model doesn't — and answers them not in theory but in practice, in businesses that have operated for decades, in real markets, with real consequences.

The questions it answers are: who captures the gains from collective effort? Who has a voice in the decisions that govern their working life? And what does a business look like when its primary stakeholders are the people who do the work?

---

Mondragon is the inevitable reference point in any serious discussion of worker cooperatives, and I want to use it carefully — neither as a romance nor as a dismissal.

Mondragon is a network of worker cooperatives founded in the Basque region of Spain in 1956, now employing roughly 80,000 worker-owners in manufacturing, retail, finance, and education. It is the world's largest cooperative corporation and one of the most studied examples of an alternative economic model actually operating at significant scale.

What Mondragon demonstrates: that democratic worker ownership can compete in global markets, can achieve quality and innovation, can sustain itself across multiple generations, and can distribute its benefits in ways that produce dramatically lower inequality than comparable conventional firms. The pay ratio between the highest and lowest earners at Mondragon has historically been capped — initially at 3:1, now closer to 6:1 or higher as the network has expanded and internationalized, but still far below the 300:1 ratios common in large American corporations.

What Mondragon does not demonstrate: that the cooperative model can simply be exported from its specific cultural and historical context. Mondragon emerged from a specific moment — post-Civil War Basque regionalism, a charismatic Catholic priest (José María Arizmendiarrieta) who understood cooperative economics as a form of social Catholicism, a community with strong traditions of mutual support and technical education. These were not incidental conditions. They were the soil in which the cooperative grew.

This does not mean cooperatives can only exist in Basque Country. It means that the cooperative model requires context — requires communities and institutions that support it — and that transplanting the form without the soil produces different results.

---

The Italian region of Emilia-Romagna provides a different, and in some ways more generalizable, example.

The cooperative sector in Emilia-Romagna accounts for roughly 40% of the regional GDP. It includes consumer cooperatives, agricultural cooperatives, construction cooperatives, and social cooperatives (which provide care services). The region has, for decades, combined high economic performance with low inequality, high quality of life, and strong civic institutions — outcomes that the conventional market model typically presents as being in tension.

The Emilia-Romagna cooperative sector did not emerge spontaneously. It was built — by the Italian communist and socialist movements of the early 20th century, by the Catholic cooperative tradition, by a regional government that understood cooperative development as economic policy, and by the cooperatives' own networks of mutual support: the Legacoop, Confcooperative, and Agci federations that provide financial services, training, and solidarity to member cooperatives.

This is the critical infrastructure that the cooperative model requires and that the dominant model usually fails to provide: networks of support, shared services, patient capital, technical assistance, and a policy environment that does not systematically disadvantage non-corporate forms.

The US cooperative sector has been growing — there are now over 400 worker cooperatives, with more forming every year — but it grows against the grain of a policy environment that was designed for conventional corporations. Access to capital is harder for cooperatives, because most investment capital requires a return structure that is incompatible with worker ownership. Technical assistance is sparse compared to small business development resources. And the very concept of worker ownership is unfamiliar enough in US business culture that recruitment, management development, and member education require resources that conventional firms can take for granted.

---

What does the cooperative difference actually feel like, on the ground?

The testimonies of worker-owners across sectors tend to cluster around several themes.

*Voice.* Not merely the right to vote on major decisions, but the experience of being genuinely consulted, of having one's expertise respected in decisions that affect one's work. In conventional employment, the worker's knowledge of their own work is often the most detailed and accurate in the organization, and is frequently ignored by the management that has authority. In cooperatives, this knowledge is structurally connected to decision-making power.

*Accountability.* When you own the business, you also own its problems. Worker-owners report a different relationship to the quality of their work — less about avoiding blame and more about genuine investment in outcomes — because the outcomes are theirs. This creates accountability that conventional management theory has been trying to replicate through incentive systems and performance reviews since Taylor, with limited success.

*Transparency.* Most cooperatives practice open-book management — all financial information is available to all members. This transparency is unusual in conventional employment, where workers are typically insulated from financial information on the assumption that they would misuse it or become anxious. The open-book cooperative finds, instead, that informed members make better decisions and are more resilient in difficult times because they understand what the difficulty actually consists of.

*Shared fate.* In conventional employment, the worker bears the risk of the business (through job insecurity) while the shareholder captures the upside. In the cooperative, the worker bears the risk and captures the upside. This is a different and, many worker-owners report, more satisfying relationship to the business's fortunes — more like partnership than transaction.

---

None of this means the cooperative model is universally superior. It means it is demonstrably viable, and it distributes power and benefit differently.

The question is not whether we should replace all conventional corporations with cooperatives tomorrow. The question is whether we want to build the conditions — the capital access, the technical assistance, the policy support, the cultural familiarity — that would allow cooperatives to compete and grow in sectors where they can genuinely serve as alternatives.

Who should capture the gains from collective effort is a question with more than one possible answer. The cooperative model is one of those answers, demonstrated in practice, across a century of evidence.

That it is not more prominent is not because it doesn't work. It's because the infrastructure of capital — the banks, the business schools, the legal frameworks, the expectations of investors — was built for a different architecture. The cooperative has to fight uphill against infrastructure designed for its opposite.

Mondragon began with five workers in a garage, in a region recovering from a civil war it had mostly lost. The infrastructure did not exist. They built it. That is the part of the story that tends not to appear in the Harvard Business Review analysis.
