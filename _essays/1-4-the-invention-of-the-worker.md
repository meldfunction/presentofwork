In September 2019, California passed AB5, a law reclassifying most gig workers as employees rather than independent contractors. Uber and Lyft responded by spending over $200 million on Proposition 22, a ballot initiative to overturn it. They won. In the process, they funded one of the most expensive ballot campaigns in California history to establish, in law, that their workers were not workers — not in the protected sense, not the kind who get minimum wage guarantees and unemployment insurance and the right to organize.

This is not a new fight. The question of who counts as a worker — who gets the protections the category carries — has been contested since the category was invented. And the category was invented, with its inclusions and exclusions, for specific purposes.

The worker did not always exist.

This sounds paradoxical — of course people have always worked, people are working right now, the word is practically definitional. But the *worker* as a legal, political, and social category — the worker as the subject of rights and protections and identities and movements — is not a natural fact. It is an invention. And like most inventions, it was built for a purpose, and that purpose shaped everything that came after.

The invention of the worker happened slowly, unevenly, and under enormous pressure. It was created by industrialists who needed to define what they were buying when they bought labor. It was shaped by reformers who needed to define who deserved protection, and by what mechanism. It was contested by workers themselves, who had complex ideas about what they were owed, which did not always align with what the law offered them. And it was built on a foundation of exclusions — of women, of people of color, of domestic workers, of those doing work that the category was designed, not accidentally, to ignore.

---

The first thing the legal category of "the worker" had to do was separate work from the worker.

In the feudal order, your labor was not a commodity you owned and could sell. It was a social obligation — owed to a lord, organized by custom and status, embedded in relationships that included but exceeded the economic. You didn't sell your working hours; you occupied a position in a social structure, and the labor was part of what that position entailed.

The emergence of the wage relation — the exchange of labor-time for money — required a prior conceptual move: the worker had to be understood as an individual, free to contract, in possession of a commodity (their time and capacity) that could be sold. This is the legal framework of "free labor" that underlies both classical liberalism and contract law.

The freedom involved was real, and it was narrow.

You were free to sell your labor to any employer. You were free, in the famous phrase, to starve in the gutter if none would have you. The freedom was a freedom to participate in a market. It was not a freedom to organize that market, to refuse its terms collectively, to withdraw your time without being replaced by someone more desperate. The freedom of labor was the freedom of the side of the market that holds the least power.

---

The labor movement of the 19th century was, at its core, a campaign to expand that narrow freedom into something more substantive. And the way it did so was precisely by constructing a political identity around the category of "the worker."

The British trade unions of the 1830s and 1840s, the American labor movement of the Gilded Age, the syndicalist movements of France and Spain — all of these built their power by creating solidarity among people who were, by the logic of the market, competitors. You are a worker. I am a worker. We share a structural position in relation to the person who owns the machines, and that shared position is the basis of a shared identity and a shared political project.

This was powerful. It won things. The eight-hour day. Child labor restrictions. Workplace safety regulation. The right to organize. Unemployment insurance. The weekend. These are not small achievements. They are the achievements of a political movement that decided that "the worker" was a category worth fighting for.

But the category came with built-in limits.

---

The most consequential limit was the definition of what counted as work.

The labor movement organized around the male industrial worker — the miner, the factory hand, the railway worker, the skilled tradesman. These were the workers whose interests were represented at the bargaining table, whose rights were codified in law, whose unions accumulated political power. The movement's definition of "the worker" was, in practice, far narrower than its rhetoric implied.

Women who worked in the home — cooking, cleaning, nursing, raising children — were not workers in the political-economic sense. Their labor did not produce a wage. It did not appear in any national account. It sustained the male worker who went out to earn the wage, and it reproduced the next generation of workers, but in the accounting of the industrial economy, it was invisible. Not because it wasn't work — everyone understood that it was work — but because it didn't fit the category as it had been built.

Silvia Federici's account of this in *Caliban and the Witch* is still the sharpest telling of how this happened. The enclosure of the commons that drove people off communal land and into the wage economy also enclosed the reproductive labor of women — domesticated it, privatized it, withdrew it from the commons of public life and confined it to the home, where it could be performed without cost to the employer and without status in the political economy. This was not an accident. It was a structural requirement of the system. The wage worker had to be reproduced. Someone had to do that work. And that someone had to be doing it for free, or the entire model stopped working.

---

The other major exclusion was race.

The category of "the worker" — even as it expanded through the late 19th and early 20th centuries to include women, to include immigrants, to include the previously excluded — consistently reproduced racial hierarchies within it. The American labor movement's relationship to Black workers is a long and largely shameful story of exclusion: unions that barred Black members, apprenticeship programs that refused Black applicants, craft workers who violently resisted the integration of their trades. The AFL under Samuel Gompers declared itself in favor of "workers" while tacitly accepting, and sometimes actively enforcing, a definition of "workers" that kept its benefits white.

Cedric Robinson's concept of racial capitalism is relevant here: the argument is not simply that capitalism is racist in its effects, but that it is constitutively racial — that the system requires the differentiation of people by race precisely in order to maintain the differentiated price of labor. If all workers were equally workers, all workers would need to be paid the same. The racial hierarchy is not a deviation from market logic; it is the market logic, applied to labor.

The legal category of "the worker," built to protect some from the worst of the market, was simultaneously a machine for specifying who counted and who didn't — who could access protection and who was left outside, available for cheaper extraction.

---

We are still inside this history.

The domestic worker who cleans your house, who is not covered by labor protections that cover most other workers, is outside the category — not by accident, but by design. Agricultural workers in the United States were explicitly excluded from the original National Labor Relations Act of 1935, which guaranteed most workers the right to organize — because Southern senators would not support a bill that gave Black agricultural laborers the same rights as white industrial workers.

The gig economy worker who is classified as an independent contractor rather than an employee is outside the category — and the legal battle over that classification is, at its heart, a battle over who gets to be a worker in the protected sense.

The undocumented worker who builds your house and cleans your hotel room and harvests the fruit you buy is outside the category in a different and more total way.

The parent who stays home with a child is still outside the category entirely.

And the care worker — the home health aide, the nursing assistant, the childcare worker — is inside the category technically, but paid at wages that reflect the consistent undervaluation of work that looks like what women have always done for free.

---

The invention of the worker was a genuine achievement. It created a political subject capable of winning real concessions from capital. It built institutions — unions, labor law, the welfare state — that reduced the worst violences of the market.

It also drew a line. And a great deal of what remains to be done — in labor law, in care policy, in immigration, in the economics of the gig economy, in the question of what happens when AI automates the work that defined the category — is the work of questioning where that line was drawn, and why, and whether it still makes sense to honor it.

The worker was invented. The worker can be reinvented.

The domestic worker cleaning the hotel room is not economically inactive. The undocumented farmworker is not outside the category because they lack the right paperwork. The parent raising children is not failing to contribute. These people are working. The category doesn't see them.

That is not the workers' failure. It is the category's.
