The Jetsons debuted on ABC on September 23, 1962. George Jetson worked one hour a day, two days a week, pressing a single button. His wife Jane managed the household with the help of Rosie, a robot maid. Their daughter Judy was a teenager with a flying car. The show was set in 2062.

We are closer to 2062 now than the show's creators were to the end of World War II.

Here is what's interesting. We haven't stopped making this promise. We've just updated the production design. The button became a touchscreen. The robot maid became an app. The one-hour workday became "passive income." The flying car became... actually we're still working on that one. Forty years of promised automation, and the average American works more hours than their counterparts in most wealthy countries, takes fewer vacation days, has less job security, and is more likely to die by suicide from work-related despair than at any time in recent memory.

The Jetsons lied to us. The interesting question is not that they lied. The interesting question is what the lie was for.

---

Every era produces its version of the promise.

The Industrial Revolution promised that machinery would free humans from drudgery, and in a limited, specific sense, it did — no one today spins wool by hand, which was genuinely backbreaking and repetitive work. What was not mentioned in the promise was that the humans freed from spinning wool were immediately required to operate spinning machines for twelve to fourteen hours a day, in conditions that were medically catastrophic.

The post-war prosperity of the 1950s and 1960s — the era that produced the Jetsons — promised that technology would deliver leisure to the masses. John Maynard Keynes had predicted, in a famous 1930 essay, that within a hundred years (i.e., by 2030) the economic problem would be solved: humans would be wealthy enough to need work only fifteen hours a week, and the central challenge of life would be learning to fill the time.

Keynes was right that productivity would rise dramatically. He was wrong that the gains would be distributed in the form of leisure. They were distributed, instead, in the form of income growth for those near the top of the wage distribution, and in the form of longer working hours for those in the middle. The productivity was real. The leisure was not.

---

What the future-of-work promise consistently elides is the distribution question.

Technology has always increased productivity. The question has never been whether technology can produce more output per unit of labor. It can, reliably. The question is who captures the output.

When a factory installs automated machinery that does the work of twenty people, there are several ways the resulting productivity gain can be distributed. It can flow to shareholders as profit. It can flow to consumers as lower prices. It can flow to the remaining workers as higher wages. It can flow to the displaced workers as a form of social insurance. It can flow to the government as tax revenue used for public goods.

In practice, over the past forty years in the United States and the United Kingdom, the gains from automation have flowed predominantly to capital. Worker wages have stagnated relative to productivity growth. The gap between what workers produce and what they are paid has widened continuously since roughly 1973. This is not a natural economic law. It is a political choice, made and remade across decades of labor law, tax policy, and corporate governance decisions.

The Jetsons was not a prediction. It was a fantasy. And fantasies are not neutral — they reveal the desires and anxieties of their moment. The fantasy of the automated home revealed a desire for middle-class comfort at the cost of acknowledging that someone had to do the work that the robots were displacing. Rosie the maid was a robot because the show's creators could not comfortably depict a human domestic worker in a show that wanted to present the Jetson family as sympathetic. The fantasy of the one-hour workday revealed a desire for leisure without examining the labor relations that would have to change for leisure to be distributed to those who most needed it.

---

The contemporary version of the Jetsons is generative AI.

The AI companies are making the same promise that the automation companies of every previous era made: this technology will free you from drudgery, will do the tedious parts of your work, will allow you to focus on the creative and meaningful and irreplaceable human elements of what you do. Some version of this is true. There are tasks that current AI systems perform well, and performing them relieves humans of effort.

The distribution question is, again, not being asked.

If AI allows a single copywriter to produce the work that previously required ten copywriters, and the company responds by employing one copywriter at their previous wage, the other nine copywriters are not freed for more creative work. They are unemployed. If AI allows a customer service platform to handle fifty thousand inquiries without human agents, the customer service workers whose jobs were replaced are not promoted to more interesting positions. They are, typically, let go.

This is not an argument against AI. It is an argument against the fantasy that AI, by itself, produces the outcomes that are being promised. The technology produces productivity. The distribution of that productivity is a political question, and it has a political answer — which depends on the relative power of workers and capital, the strength of labor law, the tax treatment of automation, the existence or absence of social insurance, and a dozen other variables that have nothing to do with the technology itself.

The Jetsons assumed that productivity growth would naturally become leisure. Every era since has assumed the same thing. Every era since has been wrong, for the same reason: productivity growth becomes leisure for the people who own the machines, and anxiety for the people who are replaced by them, until and unless someone intervenes to distribute the gains differently.

---

There is something deeper in the fantasy, though, that I want to attend to before we move on.

The one-hour workday is not just a fantasy of leisure. It is a fantasy of meaninglessness. George Jetson pressed a button. What did he make? What did he contribute? What did he do that was irreplaceable? The show didn't know, and it didn't need to — the point was that the button got pressed and he got paid and everything worked out. But this is a fantasy about the elimination of work as meaning, as much as it is a fantasy about the elimination of work as toil.

We have not, collectively, figured out what humans are for in a world where machines do most of what machines can do. The Protestant work ethic told us we were for our work, that our calling was our identity, that our productivity was a sign of our grace. Strip away the work, and you are left with a question that the culture has not answered: what, then, is a human life for?

The Jetsons didn't try to answer this question. It just showed George pressing his button and then going to the country club.

The AI companies are not trying to answer this question either. They are not in the business of philosophy. They are in the business of productivity software.

But the question is real. And it will not be answered by the technology.
