There is a bakery in my mind that I return to when I need to think clearly about what I am actually arguing for.

It employs eleven people. They are all worker-owners. They make bread, pastry, and a very good focaccia. They have been open for nine years. They have a weekly meeting at which decisions are made collectively, which sometimes takes longer than it would if one person were simply in charge. They pay a living wage. The highest-paid person earns about three times what the lowest-paid person earns. They close on Sundays.

They are not changing the world. They are not a scalable model for the reorganization of global capitalism. They are eleven people who have found a way to work together that they find dignified, and that produces good bread.

I think this matters.

---

One of the persistent temptations in writing about cooperative economics, about alternatives to the dominant model, about plural futures — is to operate at the scale of the world. To think in terms of the movements and the policies and the structural changes that would be required to genuinely transform the conditions under which most people work.

This is important work. Without structural change, cooperative workplaces are islands in a sea of conventional capitalism — demonstrating what's possible without the political power to make it general. The policy agenda matters: labor law reform, patient capital for cooperatives, regulation of platform extraction, universal care infrastructure. These things require organizing at scale, over decades, across coalitions that don't yet exist.

But the policy agenda is not the only thing that matters. And it can become, if it monopolizes attention, a way of deferring the question of what is actually possible right now, in the specific circumstances of specific people in specific communities.

Small cooperativism — the cooperative or collective enterprise at the human scale, the neighborhood, the size of people who can have a weekly meeting and know each other's names — is the unit at which the alternative becomes livable, before it becomes structural. It is where the principles of shared ownership, democratic governance, and embedded values get tested against the reality of actual human beings trying to make things and serve people and pay their bills.

---

The history of the cooperative movement is full of small cooperatives, and most of them have not survived. This is worth saying clearly. Small cooperatives fail at higher rates than conventional small businesses, for reasons that include: the governance overhead of democratic decision-making, the difficulty of accessing capital without equity investors, the limited economies of scale, the challenge of member development and education, and the absence of the kind of ecosystem support that conventional businesses receive from banks and consultants and business schools.

Knowing this is not an argument against small cooperatives. It is an argument for understanding what they require to survive.

What they require, beyond the normal requirements of any small business, is: clarity about governance (the rules for decision-making, conflict resolution, and member exit need to be designed in advance, not improvised in crisis), access to cooperative-specific technical assistance (accountants and lawyers who understand the structure, lenders who can offer patient capital), and connection to a broader network of cooperative support (so that when things go wrong, there is somewhere to turn that isn't the conventional business world).

In cities where this ecosystem exists — San Francisco, Cleveland, New York, the Pioneer Valley of Massachusetts — small cooperatives form and survive at higher rates. The ecosystem is not a miracle. It is infrastructure.

---

What does small cooperativism look like in practice, beyond the worker co-op?

**Mutual aid networks** — the neighborhood structures that appeared with particular force during the COVID-19 pandemic — are a form of small cooperativism. They are not businesses. They are not institutions. They are communities organized around the principle of *I have; you need; we share*. They are re-enactments of the *minka* and the *ayni*, the communal labor forms that the industrial economy failed to replace.

Mutual aid networks emerged in neighborhoods across the US, UK, and elsewhere in 2020, coordinating groceries for isolated elders, prescriptions for immunocompromised neighbors, childcare for essential workers. They required no formal structure and no funding. They required only the recognition that people in a place had both needs and capacities, and that the match between them could be organized without a market.

Many of them dissolved when the acute crisis passed. Some became more permanent institutions. The ones that persisted did so, generally, by taking on a slightly more structured form: a regular meeting, a shared communications infrastructure, some division of ongoing roles. They discovered the same thing that every cooperative discovers: that democratic organization requires more intentional design than hierarchical organization, but produces more resilient and more committed participation.

**Community supported agriculture (CSA)** is a form of small cooperativism between farmers and eaters: members pay at the beginning of the season for a share of what the farm produces, sharing the risk of the harvest with the farmer rather than leaving the farmer to bear it alone. The relationship is embedded — you know the farmer, they know you, the quality of the food and the conditions of its production are legible in a way that the supermarket supply chain is not.

**Tool libraries, food forests, seed libraries, repair cafes** — these are instances of the commons at the neighborhood scale: shared resources, maintained by collective effort, available to members without market pricing. They are not economically transformative in any grand sense. They are demonstrations that humans can organize the use of resources collectively, that the commons can be managed, that the tragedy of the commons (the degradation of shared resources by self-interested individuals) is not a law of nature but a failure of governance.

---

The small cooperative matters for a reason beyond what it produces or how efficiently it produces it.

It is where people learn how to be in genuine economic relationship with each other — how to make decisions together, how to handle conflict, how to distribute surplus, how to hold each other accountable, how to care for the institution they share.

These are skills. They are not natural. They are learned, through practice, in the specific conditions of actual shared enterprises with real stakes and real tensions and real humans on the other side of the table who have to agree before anything can move.

The worker who has been in a collective meeting where consensus was required, and frustrating, and eventually achieved — that person has learned something about how humans actually make decisions together that no management course teaches. The member of a food co-op who has served on the finance committee and had to explain to members why prices are rising — that person has learned something about economic literacy and civic obligation that no economics textbook provides.

Mondragon did not begin as a network of 80,000 worker-owners in global markets. It began with five workers and a garage. The Emilia-Romagna cooperative sector did not emerge fully formed. It was built, over generations, by people who started small and created the infrastructure that made building slightly larger possible.

The small cooperative is not the destination. It is the school.

The bakery in my mind has been open for nine years. It has had hard years. There was a year when the lease nearly killed them and they had to have the meeting about whether to close, and they had it together — all eleven of them, at a table, with the numbers in front of them — and they decided to stay. That meeting, with its difficulty and its honesty and its outcome, is something none of them could have had in a conventional job. The decision was theirs. The risk was theirs. The bakery is theirs.

That is a small thing. It is also not a small thing.
