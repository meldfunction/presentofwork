Yanis Varoufakis named it "technofeudalism" in his 2023 book of the same name, and the name is useful because it captures something about the current moment that neither "capitalism" nor "monopoly" quite reaches.

In the feudal order, the lord owned the land. Everyone who needed to use the land — to grow food, to build shelter, to live — paid rent. The rent did not purchase ownership. It purchased access. The peasant who paid rent did not accumulate capital. They maintained access to the resource that the lord had enclosed.

In the technofeudal order, the platform owns the infrastructure of digital life. Everyone who needs to use it — to sell products, to reach customers, to find work, to communicate, to exist socially — pays rent. Sometimes the rent is money (the commission, the subscription, the listing fee). Sometimes it is data (the behavioral record, the attention, the social network). But the structure is the same: access is rented, not owned. Participation does not accumulate equity. And the rents flow to the infrastructure owner regardless of what value is being produced by those using the infrastructure.

This is the condition we are inside. The question is what it means to resist it.

---

I want to distinguish between resistance at two scales, because they require different things and should not be collapsed into each other.

At the individual and organizational scale: the choices that individual workers, freelancers, small businesses, and organizations can make about which infrastructure they use, how they negotiate their terms, and what they build.

At the structural scale: the political and regulatory choices that determine the rules of the road — antitrust, data governance, platform regulation, collective bargaining rights for gig workers, taxation of platform rents.

Both scales matter. Neither substitutes for the other. The person who moves their newsletters off Substack onto their own domain has made a meaningful choice about platform dependency. They have not solved the structural problem of platform power. The politician who breaks up Google has addressed a structural problem. They have not addressed the individual choices that people make every day about which platforms to feed.

---

At the individual and organizational scale, anti-technofeudalism looks like a series of practices that are easy to describe and moderately difficult to maintain against the convenience gradient.

**Own your audience.** The platform's power over creators and small businesses comes primarily from its control over access to the audience. The creator on Instagram, the seller on Amazon, the driver on Uber — all have built their economic relationship on an infrastructure they don't own and could be removed from at any time. The counterpractice is to build direct relationships: email lists, websites on your own domain, relationships that survive platform migration.

This is not a radical practice. It is basic risk management, applied to platform dependency. And it is harder than it sounds, because the platforms are designed to make building on them easy and building off them difficult.

**Federate.** The technical infrastructure of the internet was built on federated protocols — systems where anyone can run a server that interoperates with everyone else's. Email works this way: your Gmail talks to my Protonmail talks to the university's Exchange server. The web works this way: any server can host a website that any browser can access. The social media era replaced this federation with centralized silos — Facebook, Instagram, Twitter/X — where the platform owns the social graph and controls interoperability.

Federated social networks (Mastodon, Bluesky's AT Protocol, Pixelfed) are rebuilding the federated layer. They are smaller than the centralized platforms. They require slightly more technical engagement to set up. And they are not subject to algorithmic manipulation in service of advertising revenue, because their business model is not advertising. The choice to participate in federated rather than centralized social infrastructure is a small act of anti-technofeudalism that also happens to produce a better social media experience for many people.

**Use and support the commons.** Wikipedia, open-source software, open-access academic publishing, creative commons licensing — the digital commons is a substantial and functional alternative to proprietary information infrastructure. Using it rather than its proprietary equivalents — LibreOffice rather than Microsoft Office, Firefox rather than Chrome, Signal rather than WhatsApp — is both a practical choice and a political one. It is also, in most cases, a good-enough practical choice for most purposes.

---

At the organizational scale, anti-technofeudalism in the platform economy means building the governance structures that allow workers who use platforms to negotiate collectively.

The challenge is that the "independent contractor" legal classification — which most platform companies impose on their workers — makes collective bargaining legally inaccessible in the United States. The National Labor Relations Act protects employees; it does not protect independent contractors. This is why Uber and Lyft fought so hard and spent so much money on Proposition 22 in California — the ballot initiative that reclassified gig workers as independent contractors after a state law had reclassified them as employees. The independent contractor classification is not just about avoiding benefit costs. It is about avoiding collective bargaining.

But workers are finding ways around the classification.

The New York City Taxi Workers Alliance, which organized taxi and app-based drivers before it was formally recognized as a union, demonstrated that organizing in industries with contractor classification is possible — it requires different tactics (sustained community organizing rather than NLRB elections) but it can produce real results (the NYC minimum earnings standard for app-based drivers, which went into effect in 2019, was won through this organizing).

The domestic workers movement — organized around the National Domestic Workers Alliance — has won domestic workers' bills of rights in multiple states, establishing labor protections for workers who were explicitly excluded from the original New Deal protections. This is slow, state-by-state, imperfect progress. It is also real.

The Freelancers Union represents independent workers who are not employees — not in the traditional union sense, which requires employment, but in the sense of providing collective access to services (health insurance, legal resources, professional development) and advocating for policy changes that affect independent workers' conditions.

---

At the structural scale, anti-technofeudalism requires political power that workers in platform economies do not yet have in most countries.

Platform regulation — requiring interoperability, limiting data use, treating platforms as public utilities — is being attempted in the European Union through the Digital Markets Act and Digital Services Act. The US has made less progress, though antitrust actions against Google, Meta, and Apple are proceeding in the courts.

Collective bargaining rights for gig workers are being expanded in some jurisdictions — the EU's Platform Work Directive requires member states to create mechanisms for platform workers to negotiate collectively. The Biden administration attempted regulatory changes to make worker classification harder to manipulate; these are contested and may not survive.

None of this is sufficient. None of it is as fast as the growth of the platform economy. But the political contestation is real and ongoing.

---

What anti-technofeudalism as practice requires, ultimately, is a recognition that the convenience of the platform comes at a price — and that the price is borne by those with the least power to negotiate the terms.

The Amazon Prime consumer who gets two-day delivery is not paying the cost of that delivery. The cost is paid by the warehouse worker whose bathroom breaks are timed and whose injury rate is three times the industry average. The Airbnb guest who gets a convenient apartment is not paying the cost of the housing market distortion. The cost is paid by the long-term residents who can no longer afford to live in their neighborhood.

What anti-technofeudalism as practice requires, ultimately, is a recognition that the convenience of the platform comes at a price — and that the price is borne by those with the least power to negotiate the terms.

This is not a comfortable recognition. The person who understands it still opens Uber. Still buys from Amazon. Still uses the tools that are designed to capture their attention and sell it. The system is convenient in ways that are very difficult to refuse unilaterally, and anyone who tells you individual refusal is sufficient is probably not doing the math on the scale of what would need to change.

Which is why refusal alone was never the point. The point is to refuse at the individual scale while building, simultaneously, the political power and the alternative infrastructure that would make the refusal generalizable. The two things are not in tension. The person who moves their newsletter to their own domain and who votes for platform regulation is doing two things that belong together.

The question is not whether you're doing enough. It's whether the things you're doing are pointed in the same direction.
