The building at 55 Wall Street in Lower Manhattan is currently a luxury event venue. In the late 18th century, it was the Tontine Coffee House — the gathering place of New York's merchants, and, from its founding in 1792, the birthplace of what would become the New York Stock Exchange.

Wall Street itself takes its name from a wall that the Dutch colonial government of New Amsterdam built in 1653 to protect its settlement. The enslaved people who built it also labored in the colony's fields and wharves, cleared the land, and generated the agricultural surplus that funded the city's commercial growth. This is not a metaphor. The literal foundations of American finance were built by enslaved labor, on land taken from the Lenape people, along a street named for the fortification that divided the colony from the territory beyond.

History very rarely offers this degree of clarity. When it does, it is worth looking directly at.

---

The relationship between chattel slavery and the rise of industrial capitalism is not a story about distant moral failures contaminating an otherwise clean system. It is a story about structural dependencies — about the specific financial and institutional arrangements without which the Industrial Revolution, as it actually occurred, could not have occurred in the form it did.

Edward Baptist's *The Half Has Never Been Told* opens this argument most clearly for an American audience. The cotton that fueled the textile mills of Lancashire and Lowell came overwhelmingly from the American South, from plantations worked by enslaved people whose forced labor produced a commodity at a price that the market for free labor could not have matched. The price of American cotton was subsidized by the unpaid labor of millions of human beings over two centuries. The Industrial Revolution ran, in large part, on that subsidy.

The financial architecture was equally direct. Enslaved people were collateral. They could be mortgaged, insured, and used as security for loans. The Louisiana and Mississippi plantation economy was financed, substantially, by bond issues underwritten by banks in New York and London — bonds backed by the bodies of the people who worked the land. Insurance companies wrote policies on enslaved people as property. Actuarial tables calculated their expected productivity, their mortality risk, their value at various ages. The financial instruments of the modern economy were being invented and refined in the process of financializing human beings.

Lloyd's of London insured slave ships. American banks whose institutional descendants still operate extended credit to plantation owners against their human collateral. Prestigious universities in the United Kingdom and the United States were endowed, in part, by the fortunes of slave merchants and slave owners. The sugar that sweetened British tea was produced by enslaved people in the Caribbean. The cotton that clothed industrial workers was produced by enslaved people in the American South.

---

The plantation, in this history, is not an anachronism — a survival of a pre-modern form of social organization into the industrial era. It is a modern institution. It is, as historians have increasingly argued, a proto-factory.

The plantation organized labor with a precision and a managerial intensity that was genuinely novel in its time. Edward Baptist documents the "pushing system" — the practice of plantation managers tracking each enslaved person's daily cotton output, setting the next day's minimum above the current day's achievement, and responding to any shortfall with violence. This was not merely cruelty. It was an early form of systematic labor intensification — the use of quantified performance targets, combined with punitive enforcement, to extract the maximum possible output from each worker's body and time.

Frederick Winslow Taylor, the father of "scientific management," developed his time-motion studies of factory workers in the late 19th century and was celebrated as a revolutionary innovator. The plantation had been conducting analogous studies, with more violent enforcement mechanisms, for over a century. Taylor's contribution was to apply the plantation's intensification logic to free labor. The machinery was different. The principle was the same.

This is not to say that the plantation and the factory were the same thing — the specific horror of chattel slavery was not its labor intensification but its total denial of the humanity of the people it exploited. What the comparison reveals is that the managerial logic, the quantitative approach to human effort, the idea that the purpose of management is to extract the maximum possible output from the minimum possible investment in workers, did not begin in the factory. It was refined in the plantation.

---

The financial legacy of slavery is also not historical. It is present.

The wealth that was accumulated by slave merchants, slave owners, and the industries that depended on enslaved labor did not disappear with emancipation. It was inherited — by families, by institutions, by regions, by nations. The City of London's financial district grew rich on the trade. The South's plantation wealth was partly destroyed by the Civil War, and then reconstituted in different forms after Reconstruction. The industries that were built on slave-produced raw materials — textile manufacturing, insurance, finance — became the industries that defined 20th-century capitalism.

The dispossession was also inherited. The people who had been enslaved were freed without land, without capital, without the forty acres and a mule that would have represented a minimal material foundation for economic independence. They entered the free labor market from a position of absolute disadvantage — no savings, no property, no political rights, and in a social structure violently committed to maintaining their subordination.

The economist Thomas Piketty's analysis of wealth inequality shows that once wealth is concentrated, it tends to remain concentrated — the return on capital exceeds the rate of economic growth, so those who start with more end up with relatively more. The starting point of the post-emancipation Black community — stripped of every economic resource, entering a labor market that was immediately resegregated by law and violence — was not just low. It was deliberately designed to be the lowest possible point from which to begin.

A study by economists in 2020 found that enslaved Americans produced, in aggregate, approximately $14 trillion in unrecompensated labor. That figure, and the wealth it represents, was not destroyed at emancipation. It was transferred — to those who had held the enslaved people as property, to the financial institutions that had financed their trade, to the governments that had taxed their productivity. The beneficiaries are not all identifiable by name. But they are, in aggregate, identifiable. And so are the people who were owed what was taken.

---

The history of slavery's relationship to capitalism is not a history that can be kept in a separate chapter, labeled "atrocity" or "tragedy" and moved past. It is a history of the specific material and institutional conditions that made the world we currently inhabit. The financial instruments, the managerial practices, the trade networks, the accumulated capital, the impoverishment of specific communities — these are not background. They are the foreground of the present.

The story of how selling your time became the definition of a good life cannot be told honestly without including the story of the people whose time was never theirs to sell. The story of the Industrial Revolution cannot be told honestly without the cotton. The story of Wall Street cannot be told honestly without the Tontine Coffee House on the street built by the enslaved.

This is not guilt. Guilt is the wrong frame. Guilt is a private emotion about personal responsibility. This is a question about structural inheritance — about what we are living inside, who built it, and what was extracted to fund the building.

The present of work is built on those foundations.

That is not an argument for despair. It is an argument for accuracy — for seeing the structure clearly enough to know what would need to change for the structure to be just.
