There is a word — *gelassenheit* — used in Anabaptist and Quaker traditions to describe a quality of spiritual submission or yielding: a release of the ego's demands, a willingness to be present without grasping. It is not passivity. It is a form of attending to the world without the distorting lens of what you want to get from it.

I start with this word because I want to contrast it with a tendency in contemporary life that is its structural opposite: the relentless conversion of human qualities into commodities. The transformation of presence into content. Attention into product. Care into service. Relationship into network asset. Trust into brand value.

This conversion is not incidental to the modern economy. It is one of its central dynamics. And it has consequences for the humans being commodified that we have barely begun to understand.

---

The attention economy is the most visible example.

When social media platforms say they offer their services "for free," what they mean is that you pay with your attention, and they sell that attention to advertisers. The product is you — your time, your engagement, your behavioral data. The attention economy is not a metaphor. It is a market in which human attentiveness is the traded good.

What this creates: an incentive to design products that maximize attention, regardless of whether that attention is in the interest of the person paying it. The techniques that capture attention most reliably — variable reward schedules, social validation loops, the outrage amplification that keeps people engaged through anger — are not techniques that promote human flourishing. They are techniques that exploit human psychology for commercial gain.

The philosopher Simone Weil, writing in the 1940s, made an argument that attention is not merely a cognitive skill but a moral and spiritual one — that the capacity for genuine, patient, loving attention to another person or to a task is one of the highest human achievements, and one that can be cultivated or destroyed by the conditions in which we live. Her essay "Attention and Will" is not about the internet — it predates the internet by decades — but it describes precisely what the attention economy destroys: the capacity for sustained, voluntary, non-compelled attentiveness to anything.

If Weil is right that attention is a form of love — that to genuinely attend to something is a form of care — then an economy designed to fragment and capture attention is an economy that is, in a precise sense, destroying a human capacity for care.

---

The commodification of care itself is a related and older story.

Care work — the work of tending to human beings who are vulnerable, dependent, or in need — has always existed at the intersection of the economic and the relational. A parent cares for a child, and this care is primarily a relationship rather than a service. A nurse cares for a patient, and the relationship is inseparable from the clinical function. A teacher cares for a student, and the care is part of what makes the teaching possible.

When these relationships are subjected to market logic — when care is systematized, measured, optimized, and priced — something changes in the relationship. Not because the care disappears, but because the conditions under which care can be genuinely given are progressively eroded.

The nurse who has to care for nine patients simultaneously, because the ratio has been set by a cost model rather than a care model, is not giving bad care because she is a bad nurse. She is giving compromised care because the system has stripped away the conditions under which the care she is capable of could be delivered. The care has been commodified — broken into measurable units, priced, and delivered at the quantity that the pricing model allows — and in the process, it has been degraded.

This is what Arlie Hochschild documented in the managed heart: the commercialization of feeling. But it extends beyond the emotional management of the service worker to the structural conditions of all caring relationships. When care becomes a product, it loses something essential — the quality of presence that makes care healing rather than merely functional.

---

The commodification of creativity follows a similar pattern.

The contemporary creator economy promises independent creative workers access to global audiences and direct compensation from fans who value their work. This is genuinely new and genuinely significant — the musician who once required a record label to reach an audience can now build one directly. The writer who once required a publisher can self-publish. The visual artist who once required a gallery can sell through their Instagram.

But the creator economy is also a system that converts creative work into content, and content into a performance metric, and the performance metric into income — and in doing so, subjects the creative process to the logic of engagement optimization.

The creator who makes content is operating under different incentives than the artist who makes art. The content creator asks: what will perform? What will be shared? What will drive views, follows, subscriptions, and revenue? These are not the same questions as: what is true? What is beautiful? What needs to be said? What do I understand that I can only express this way?

This is not to say that the creator economy is simply bad. For many creators, especially those previously excluded from mainstream channels, it has been genuinely liberating. But the system creates pressures — toward the legible over the challenging, the viral over the durable, the personal brand over the authentic voice — that shape creative work in ways that serve the platform's interest in engagement more than the artist's interest in expression.

The artist who becomes an influencer is not the same thing as an artist. The care worker who becomes a productivity content creator is not the same thing as a care worker. The conversion is real, and it costs something.

---

Marx called this process "alienation" — the separation of the worker from the product of their labor, from the process of production, from other workers, and from their own human essence. What is being described here is a related but distinct phenomenon: the conversion of the human *into* a commodity, rather than merely the conversion of their labor. When your personality is a brand, your relationships are a network, your attention is a product, your care is a service — you are not just selling your labor. You are selling yourself, in a more total way.

This is the logical endpoint of the marketization of everything: not just that goods and services are bought and sold, but that the humans who produce and consume them are themselves converted into inputs and outputs of the market system.

The resistance to this conversion takes many forms. It can be deliberate privacy — the refusal to perform one's interior life online. It can be the cultivation of relationships that are explicitly non-transactional — friendships, communities, practices that are valued precisely because they resist pricing. It can be work that is done not for an audience or a market but for the intrinsic satisfaction of doing it well.

These resistances are not solutions to a structural problem. They are, in the conditions available to individuals, forms of practice — ways of maintaining a relationship to one's own humanity that the market has not yet fully colonized.

What would constitute a solution is a different question, and probably a political one: about who owns the platforms, who governs the algorithms, who sets the conditions under which human attention and care and creativity circulate, and whether those conditions can be set in ways that serve the humans in the system rather than the system's owners.

The human is not a product. That this needs to be said is itself an indication of how far the conversion has progressed.
