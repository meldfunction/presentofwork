God, in the pre-Reformation Catholic view, was not particularly interested in your job.

He was interested in your soul, which was the project of the church — maintained through sacrament, confession, prayer, pilgrimage, and the various other technologies of salvation that the medieval church offered. Your work was part of your temporal life, which was a waiting room, a staging area, the realm of necessary suffering through which you passed on your way to the only thing that mattered. Work could be offered to God, as a monk's labor in the scriptorium was offered. But it was not inherently sacred. It was not itself a calling. It was what the body did while the soul waited for release.

Martin Luther changed this in 1520, with a sermon that would reshape the relationship between religion and labor for the next five centuries.

The concept Luther introduced — and it is a word worth examining — was *Beruf*.

---

*Beruf* is usually translated as "calling" or "vocation." But the translation is inadequate, because in German, *rufen* means to call, and *Beruf* carries the sense that God is the caller, that your work in the world is a divine summons, that the cobbler who makes shoes is serving God as surely as the monk who chants the hours.

This sounds, from a secular distance, like a minor theological adjustment. It was not. It was a revolution in the social location of the sacred.

Before Luther, the highest calling was to leave the world — to enter the monastery, to withdraw from the cycle of earthly labor and social obligation, and to dedicate the whole of one's life to contemplation and prayer. The monk was the exemplary Christian. The laboring peasant was, spiritually speaking, a lesser grade of being, doing necessary but undignified work in the fallen world.

After Luther, every calling was equally a calling. The cobbler's Beruf was not inferior to the monk's. God had placed each person in their station, and faithful performance of that station's duties was itself worship. Work was not an unfortunate byproduct of the Fall. It was the site of the Christian life.

Max Weber, in *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism* (1905), traced what this meant in practice, and his argument — though contested in its specifics — points to something real. If your work is your calling, then how you work becomes a moral question. Diligence, sobriety, and productivity are not merely practical virtues — they are expressions of your relationship to God. Leisure becomes spiritually suspect. Idleness becomes sin. And accumulation — since you are forbidden to spend on yourself what the Puritan God forbids — becomes evidence of election.

---

Weber's thesis was that this theological framework was one of the preconditions for capitalist accumulation. Not the only cause. Not even the primary cause. But one of the cultural conditions that made the spirit of capitalism feel not merely profitable, but righteous.

You could work for a God who wanted you to work. You could accumulate for a God who took accumulation as evidence of grace. You could look at the poor and see not the face of Christ, as the Catholic tradition had urged, but evidence of insufficient virtue, of a calling poorly answered, of a person whom God, in his wisdom, had not particularly favored.

This is not a small theological move. It is the move that makes modern meritocracy possible — the belief that your position in the economic hierarchy reflects your worth, your effort, your character. It is the move that makes poverty a personal failure rather than a structural condition. It is the move that makes "hard work" a virtue available to all, in theory, while the structural conditions that determine who succeeds at it are ignored.

The Calvinist doctrine of predestination — the belief that God had already determined who was saved and who was damned — produced, in practice, an extraordinary anxiety. How do you know you are among the elect? You look for signs. And in the Calvinist communities of 17th-century England and America, the sign that tended to be read as most legible was worldly success. The person who thrived in their calling was likely to be among the saved. The person who struggled was likely to be among the damned.

This is the theology underneath the prosperity gospel. Underneath the hustle culture. Underneath the entire apparatus of the "deserving poor" versus the "undeserving poor." Underneath the strange American fusion of religious conviction and economic ambition that has produced some of the most productive and some of the most spiritually impoverished people in the world.

---

Luther and Calvin did not intend these consequences, and history rarely works by straight lines.

Luther's concept of *Beruf* was meant to honor the dignity of ordinary labor, to resist the hierarchy that placed the contemplative above the active life, to insist that God was present in the workshop and the kitchen as much as in the monastery. This is a genuinely beautiful idea. It is the idea that the nurse making a bed, the teacher explaining long division, the farmer marking the turning of the season — that these are not lesser activities than the philosopher's inquiry or the priest's prayer. That all honest work participates in the divine project.

That idea is worth keeping.

What it got entangled with — the notion that success in your calling signals divine favor, that poverty signals divine disfavor, that the market is a kind of providential mechanism for sorting the virtuous from the vicious — is the corruption of that idea. And the corruption is so deeply woven into the fabric of modern work culture that it is often invisible to the people inside it.

---

There is a concept in Sanskrit, *dharma*, that is sometimes translated as "duty" or "calling" and that has a structural resemblance to *Beruf* — the idea that each person has a right work suited to their nature, a proper function in the cosmic order. But dharma includes something that Beruf, in its Protestant deployment, conspicuously lacks: an acknowledgment that the order itself must be just for the duty to be meaningful.

The warrior who fights an unjust war is not fulfilling their dharma. The priest who maintains a corrupt priesthood is not fulfilling their dharma. The calling exists within a context, and the context has moral weight.

The Protestant work ethic, as it was deployed in industrial capitalism, stripped this contextual judgment out. Your calling was to do your job well, full stop. The justice of the economic system within which your job existed was not your department. God had placed you in your station. Your job was to be diligent in it.

This is a theology designed for a particular kind of compliance.

---

It produced, among other things, the phenomenon that E.P. Thompson documented in the English working class of the early industrial period: the internalization of the work ethic as a moral framework by the very people it was designed to discipline. The workers who policed each other's punctuality. The communities that identified idleness as a moral failure. The pride in labor that made the miner's dignity inseparable from his willingness to descend, every day, into conditions that were destroying his lungs.

The moral framework was real. The pride was real. The community that organized around it was real.

And it was deployed in service of a system that used that pride to extract the maximum amount of labor at the minimum price, in conditions that would now be called criminal, with outcomes in life expectancy and occupational disease that we would now describe as catastrophic.

That is the Reformation's wages: a genuine elevation of the dignity of ordinary labor, bent to the service of its exploitation. A theology of calling, used to make workers feel that their submission to the demands of capital was a form of virtue.

The theology is still with us. The word "work ethic" still carries a moral charge, as though the amount you work reveals your character. The question of whether you "deserve" your success is still asked in terms that assume the answer can be found by examining the individual's effort and virtue, rather than the structural conditions in which that effort occurred.

Luther was trying to give work back its dignity. The market turned that dignity into a mechanism of discipline.

What's strange is how thoroughly it worked. The miners who destroyed their lungs with pride. The teachers who work summers unpaid because their calling demands it. The nurses who stay two hours past the end of a shift not because the hospital asks, but because the patient needs it, and because something in their formation tells them that need is a claim on them.

The theological framework that was supposed to elevate work made it very easy to extract everything from the people who took it seriously.
