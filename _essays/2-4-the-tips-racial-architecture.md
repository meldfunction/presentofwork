Tipping in America is not a custom. It is a system. And like most systems, it was designed for specific purposes, and those purposes are visible if you look at the history.

The tip arrived in the United States from Europe in the late 19th century, where it had existed as a practice among the aristocracy — a small gratuitous payment to servants who were expected to perform their duties without complaint. American labor reformers at the time opposed tipping vigorously. The National Anti-Tipping Society, founded in 1905, argued that tipping was undemocratic, demeaning to workers, and inconsistent with American values of equality and self-sufficiency. Several states banned it between 1905 and 1916.

And then, after Emancipation, tipping became structurally important to a specific employer class, and the opposition collapsed.

---

The history that has been most thoroughly documented by Saru Jayaraman of One Fair Wage connects the American tipping system directly to the post-Civil War period. Restaurant owners and railroad companies — industries that were hiring large numbers of formerly enslaved Black workers — lobbied successfully against a guaranteed minimum wage for tipped workers, arguing that the tip itself constituted sufficient compensation.

The argument was accepted. And the federal "tipped minimum wage" — which allows employers to pay tipped workers a cash wage below the standard minimum, on the assumption that tips will make up the difference — was set in 1938 at the same level as the regular minimum wage. It has been $2.13 per hour since 1991.

This is not an accident of policy drift. It is a design. The tipped subminimum wage was created, explicitly, in the context of industries that employed Black workers and wished to avoid paying them wages equivalent to what white workers in non-tipped occupations earned. The tip was not a supplement to a living wage. The tip was the mechanism by which the employer transferred the cost of wages to customers, while also creating a system in which Black workers' income depended on the willingness of customers — often white customers, in a deeply segregated country — to compensate them adequately.

---

The contemporary tipping system has several distinctive features that trace to this origin.

First: it places workers' income at the mercy of customer discretion, which is not racially neutral. Studies consistently show that servers who are Black, or who serve Black customers, receive lower tips than servers who are white serving white customers — controlling for service quality. A worker's income depends, in part, on their race and their customers' race, in a way that no formal wage discrimination law can reach, because the customer's decision is notionally private.

Second: it is the enforcement mechanism of a particular kind of emotional labor. The worker who must perform warmth, gratitude, and attention — not because they feel these things, but because their income depends on the customer's perception — is performing emotional labor for compensation that is contingent, variable, and controlled entirely by the person being served.

Third: it creates a deeply asymmetric power relationship. The customer who controls the tip controls the worker's income. This power asymmetry explains much of what researchers have documented about tipping interactions: the tendency of servers to touch customers lightly (which increases tips), to introduce themselves by name (which increases tips), to tell customers that the special is "excellent" regardless of its actual quality (which increases tips). These are not spontaneous human behaviors. They are learned adaptations to a system in which the customer is, in a very literal economic sense, the server's employer.

---

The defense of tipping, which is vociferous and comes primarily from within the restaurant industry, takes several forms.

The most common is: servers make more in tips than they would under a guaranteed wage. This is sometimes true and sometimes not, and the average conceals enormous variance — between good nights and bad, between high-end restaurants and diners, between servers who are young, white, conventionally attractive, and working in the right establishment, and those who are not. The average also conceals the instability: a server who makes $60,000 in a good year makes nothing in a pandemic, nothing when the restaurant closes for renovations, nothing when they're sick and can't work. The guaranteed wage that seems lower in comparison provides something the tip does not: reliability.

The second defense is: servers prefer tips because they provide the possibility of high earnings. This is the same argument that is made about piece-rate work, about independent contracting, about every form of contingent compensation: that the upside potential is worth the downside risk. It is an argument that applies primarily to workers whose baseline security is high enough to bear the risk. For workers who are living paycheck to paycheck — the majority of tipped workers — the risk of a bad week is not an acceptable trade for the chance of a great one.

The third defense is more honest: the restaurant industry cannot survive mandatory living wages for tipped workers. Some version of this may be true for some restaurants. If it is, it is an argument that the current restaurant business model is subsidized by workers whose wages are below what a fair market would provide. That is a feature of the model that the owners of the model prefer not to examine.

---

The states that have eliminated the tipped subminimum wage and required all workers to be paid the standard minimum — California, Washington, Oregon, Minnesota, and others — have not seen the restaurant apocalypse that industry advocates predict. They have seen somewhat higher menu prices, somewhat different tipping patterns, and restaurant industries that function.

What they have also seen is a gradual shift in the labor dynamics of the restaurant: less dependence on the demographic characteristics of the server, more predictable income for workers, and a reduction in the specific forms of harassment that the tip relationship enables. When a customer's control over a worker's income is absolute, the worker has limited recourse when that customer's behavior crosses lines. When the wage is guaranteed, the worker has slightly more standing.

---

I want to close with the observation that the tip is a useful lens for examining the broader question of how labor is compensated in the United States.

The tipping system is an extreme version of a logic that pervades much of the service economy: that workers' compensation should be contingent on the satisfaction of the person being served, rather than guaranteed as a function of the labor performed. That the market — in the form of the customer — knows better than any collective agreement what a service is worth, and will price it accordingly.

This logic is not neutral. It consistently produces compensation that tracks the social status of the worker more closely than the quality of their work. It consistently transfers power from workers to their buyers. It consistently fails workers who are racialized, gendered, or otherwise marked as less deserving of full compensation.

The tip is not a gift. It is a wage subsidy paid by customers to a system that has decided workers don't deserve guaranteed compensation for their labor.

And the system was designed, explicitly, by people who did not think that all workers deserved the same floor.
