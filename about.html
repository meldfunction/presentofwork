A gathering of ideas, communities, and practices for all forms of work. Not about jobs. About being alive.

What this is

Language
is the work.

We talk about "the economy" and "the market" as if they are agents — things that act upon us, collapse, recover, demand sacrifice. They aren't. People are. Nature is. The moment you treat an abstraction as an agent, you stop being able to see who is actually making the decisions.

That's where this project starts: with the observation that the language we've inherited for talking about work has been doing ideological work of its own. Nouns that used to name activities have become entities with their own momentum. "Housing" instead of "people building homes for each other." "Labor" instead of "people doing things that matter." Recovering the verb is recovering the agency.

This is not a site about jobs. It is about recovering the full range of what human beings do when they contribute to the world — and finding language precise enough to name what's actually happening.

"
English is a noun-based language, somehow appropriate to a culture so obsessed with things.
— Robin Wall Kimmerer, Braiding Sweetgrass, 2013

These are not subsidiaries. They are organisms in the same ecology — different functions, different scales, different relationships to light. What holds them together is not a structure. It is shared geology: the same values, the same questions, the same refusal to treat human beings as resources to be optimized.

What we'd like to see

Eight things
worth wanting.

1
An economy that counts care. Where the work that holds everything together — raising children, tending the sick, building community — is visible, valued, and compensated.
2
Language that names agency correctly. Where we stop saying "the market decided" and start saying "these people chose this, and those people paid for it."
3
Plural economies, not one. Worker co-ops, commons governance, gift economies, mutual aid networks — coexisting, not competing for the right to be called the future.
4
Technology that asks the Amish question. Does this strengthen community bonds or weaken them? Accepts the tool only if the answer is yes.
5
Indigenous knowledge restored to its source. Starting with Maslow giving back what he took from the Blackfoot, and continuing from there.
6
The full taxonomy recognized. Subsistence, craft, care, communal, sacred, interior, political, creative — all ten types of work counted, not just the ones that generate wages.
7
A right to rest that isn't earned. Where Tricia Hersey's argument is obvious, not radical: that rest is a form of resistance, and your worth was never up for negotiation.
8
An ecology of organizations, not a hierarchy. Where different functions coexist at different scales without any of them claiming to be the center.
