The word "work" is about 250 years old — in the sense we currently use it. Before the Industrial Revolution, people labored, crafted, tended, and gathered, but they did not *work* the way we work: discretely, by the hour, for an employer, as their primary social identity.

In this first episode, we examine the transition. What did labor look like before the factory? What made the factory possible? And what did the enclosure of the commons, the discipline of the clock, and the invention of the "worker" as a legal category actually take from the people it was imposed upon?

## What we cover

- The medieval task-based workday and why "finishing early" was a right, not a reward
- The commons — what they were, how they functioned, and what their enclosure meant for ordinary people
- E.P. Thompson's *The Making of the English Working Class* and why the working class had to be made
- The difference between *ayni* and wage labor — and why Quechua has a better vocabulary for this than English
