Here's the thing about 404s.
HTTP status codes are little bureaucratic poems.
200: I found it. You're welcome. Here you go. Enjoy.
301: It moved. Permanently. Someone's whole situation changed.
403: I know it's here and I'm not telling you. Power.
500: I broke. Everything is fine. Nothing is fine. The server is on fire.
404: Gone. Maybe never existed. The void has a number and it's four, zero, four.
There's a whole philosophy here. Kathi Weeks would say the 404 is ideological. Something was supposed to be here, and it isn't, and now you feel faintly bad about it. Why? You followed a link. That's not failure. That's faith.
You believed that clicking a thing would take you somewhere useful. That's actually quite beautiful.

Shadow work. You've heard about shadow work? It's all the unpaid labour institutions generate for their users. Bagging your own groceries. Checking yourself in at the airport. Navigating endless hold music before reaching someone who also can't help you.
The 404 page is shadow work. You have to figure out where you actually wanted to go. You have to search again. You're doing the router's job because the router couldn't be bothered to redirect.

A brief history of work.
Before the Industrial Revolution, most people didn't have jobs. They had things to do. Fields, animals, children, crafts, ceremonies, obligations, relationships.
The concept of "going to work" — of separating labour time from life time — is about 200 years old. We think it's ancient. We think it's natural. We think it's the point. It's a toddler. A historically recent accident that became a religion.
And I'm telling you this here, on a 404 page, because this is arguably the most honest page on the whole site.
Every other page is trying to be something. This one just... is. No content. No agenda. No conversion funnel. Just a page, existing, wondering what you want from it.
That's extremely relatable.
The question the site asks.
The Present of Work is about recovering the verb. We turned work into a noun and then forgot it was ever anything else.
Housing instead of people building homes. Labor instead of people doing things together. The economy instead of choices made by identifiable human beings with comprehensible interests.
The noun makes it sound inevitable. Fixed. Market forces. Just the way things are. The verb gives it back to the people doing it.
I don't know why you came here. A broken link, probably. Bad copy-paste. An old bookmark. Mis-typed URL. The internet is full of trails that end here.
And usually the advice is: go back, try again, check the URL. But maybe — just maybe — the page that wasn't there is less interesting than the question of why you followed the link in the first place.
