Ten
kinds
of work.

Occupational employment is one form of work. Not the default — one form. This is a map of the rest: the ten types every community has always performed, the words other languages use that English collapsed into a single noun, and the thinkers who named what was happening.

01
Type of work
Subsistence
Llankay
Quechua — Andean civilizations
Physical work done in service of life: cultivating food, tending animals, building shelter. Not toil — an expression of relationship with the land.

Producing food, shelter, and warmth. The biological floor. Hunting, gathering, farming, foraging — still the primary work of most people on earth, yet largely invisible in the GDP of wealthy nations. When economists talk about the future of work, they almost never mean this work. They should.

The Quechua concept of llankay refuses the separation between labor and relationship with the land. Work is not extraction — it is participation. The agricultural worker in a Central Valley greenhouse and the subsistence farmer in highland Bolivia are both doing this work. One of them is counted. One is not.

02
Type of work
Craft
Werk
German
The made thing — craft, production, the artifact that outlasts the maker. Distinct from Arbeit (toil) and Beruf (vocation/calling). English collapses all three into "work."
Shokunin
Japanese
The artisan who pursues mastery as spiritual practice. Not an occupation — a relationship to excellence that gives life its structure.

Making things with skill and care. The guild tradition, the apprenticeship, the object that carries the maker's intelligence. Hannah Arendt called this "work" proper — distinct from labor (which leaves no trace) and action (which changes the political world). Craft is what builds the durable world.

Richard Sennett spent a career arguing that modern capitalism corrodes craft by eliminating the long-term commitment mastery requires. You cannot become a shokunin in a gig economy. You cannot build a guild when the contract ends in ninety days.

Richard Sennett

03
Type of work
Care
Seva
Sanskrit / Hindu, Sikh traditions
Selfless service — care given without expectation of return. The opposite of the productivity mindset: pure contribution, unmeasured.

Tending the sick, raising children, supporting the vulnerable. Silvia Federici named it reproductive labor — the work that reproduces life itself and makes all other work possible. It has always been done primarily by women. It has never been fully paid. This is not an accident. It is a structure.

Arlie Hochschild's concept of emotional labor extended this further: care work requires managing your own feelings to produce the right feelings in others. The flight attendant, the nurse, the customer service worker, the teacher — all perform emotional labor as an invisible condition of the job. That labor is real. It costs something. The market has never found a way to count it.

04
Type of work
Communal
Ayni
Quechua — Andean civilizations
Reciprocal labor: I help you build your house, you help me harvest. Neither a transaction nor a gift — a relationship of mutual obligation across time.
Minka
Quechua
Communal labor toward a shared good. The collective and the individual are not in tension — they are the same action seen from different scales.

Labor freely given to and received from community. Not transactional. The Amish barn raising is the most legible Western example: what it produces is not just a barn — it is social cohesion, demonstrated mutual obligation, renewed commitment to collective life.

Gelassenheit — the Amish concept of yielding to community will — also contains their technology philosophy: a technology is accepted only if it strengthens community bonds. Rejected if it creates hierarchy or isolation. This is not primitivism. It is a design principle. Contemporary parallels: COVID mutual aid networks, repair cafes, community land trusts, timebanks, Wikipedia.

05
Type of work
Sacred
Avodah
Hebrew
A single word meaning work, service, and worship simultaneously. The splitting apart of these three is a specifically modern, secular wound — not a universal human condition.

Maintaining the community's relationship with meaning, the cosmos, the ancestors. Ceremony, liturgy, ritual, prayer. In most of human history, this work was not separate from other work — it was the frame within which all other work made sense.

The Hebrew avodah holds the collapse point in a single word: before modernity, labor and devotion were not distinct categories. Their separation is what we inherited, not what humans have always known. The Still — the contemplative practice community forming in this ecosystem — is one attempt to work with that gap directly.

06
Type of work
Interior
Kammathāna
Pali / Buddhist tradition
Literally "basis of work" — refers to meditation objects, the ground of interior practice. The highest work is interior. Not escape from the world, but the cultivation of the mind that acts in it.

The work done on and within the self. Contemplation, meditation, therapy, the examined life. Buddhist sammā-ājīva — Right Livelihood — is one of the oldest frameworks for ethical labor: work shouldn't cause harm, shouldn't involve deception, shouldn't be purely extractive. This predates CSR by two millennia.

The Blackfoot model of the self begins here — not with hierarchy but with the assumption that self-actualization is the given, not the peak. Everyone arrives with inherent worth and creative capacity. Maslow inverted this when he abstracted it into a pyramid where self-actualization is rare and earned. That inversion became modern HR theory.

07
Type of work
Political

The labor of maintaining, contesting, and transforming collective structures. Hannah Arendt called this action — the genuinely human and irreplaceable category, the one that cannot be automated because it is constitutively relational. You cannot action alone. It requires others, and it changes things.

Organizing, advocacy, mutual aid, governance, the work of care for the commons — none of it appears in productivity statistics. All of it is what holds societies together. The labor movement built the forty-hour week. Amazon workers organized in Staten Island. Starbucks workers went union city by city. This work is not separate from economic work. It is the work that sets the conditions for all other work.

08
Type of work
Creative

Making culture, meaning, beauty. Distinct from craft in that it is not primarily functional — its function is to expand what is thinkable, sayable, feelable. Ursula K. Le Guin argued that creative work is where we rehearse different worlds before we build them. Speculative fiction as policy work.

The creative economy has a specific pathology: passion as substitute for pay, winner-take-all markets, the exposure economy where artists are grateful to work for free. Angela McRobbie named this the paradox of creative labor — the more you love it, the more exploitable you are. AI generative tools are the newest pressure point in this dynamic.

09
Type of work
Administrative

Coordinating collective activity. Necessary in any complex community. Also the category that expands to fill available time (Parkinson's Law) and generates what David Graeber called bullshit jobs — positions whose holders are secretly convinced they serve no purpose, and would not be missed if they disappeared.

Ivan Illich named the deeper mechanism: shadow work. The unpaid labor that institutions generate for users — filling forms, navigating bureaucracies, managing your own data, doing your own check-in. Every productivity app that promises to save you time generates shadow work in its administration. The net gain is often negative.

10
Type of work
Attention

The newest category. When you scroll, you are working — generating data, training models, producing behavioral signals, building the advertising inventory that platforms sell. You are not the customer. You are the labor.

Shoshana Zuboff named this surveillance capitalism: the economic logic that harvests human behavioral data as raw material for prediction products sold to advertisers. The attention economy is an uncompensated labor system at a scale no previous economic form has achieved. One billion people working for free, every day, building someone else's asset.

The question this type of work raises for Series II and III: if machines eventually do most occupational work, and human attention becomes the primary resource platforms extract, what does liberation actually look like? Leisure — or a new form of enclosure?
